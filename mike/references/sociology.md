# Sociology Models

Social dynamics, group behavior, and cultural patterns that shape human societies.

## Diffusion of Innovations

**Principle**: How new ideas and technologies spread through a population over time.

**Adopter Categories**:
- **Innovators** (2.5%): Risk-takers, first to try
- **Early Adopters** (13.5%): Opinion leaders, selective
- **Early Majority** (34%): Deliberate, need proof
- **Late Majority** (34%): Skeptical, adopt due to pressure
- **Laggards** (16%): Traditional, last to adopt

**When to Use**:
- Product launches
- Change management
- Social movement strategy
- Technology adoption prediction

**Questions**:
- Which adopter segment are we targeting?
- What drives adoption in each group?
- Have we crossed the chasm?

**Example**: Smartphones: Apple iPhone targeted innovators/early adopters, then crossed to mainstream.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all innovations succeed
- Adoption patterns vary by context
- Network effects can accelerate or impede

## Tipping Point

**Principle**: The moment when an idea, trend, or behavior crosses a threshold and spreads rapidly.

**When to Use**:
- Social change initiatives
- Viral marketing
- Epidemic modeling
- Understanding rapid shifts

**Three Rules (Malcolm Gladwell)**:
1. **Law of the Few**: Key influencers drive spread
2. **Stickiness Factor**: Memorable message
3. **Power of Context**: Environment matters

**Questions**:
- What's the critical mass?
- Who are the connectors and influencers?
- What environmental factors enable spread?

**Example**: Crime in NYC dropping rapidly; fashion trends going mainstream; social movements gaining momentum.

**Pitfalls**:
- Tipping points hard to predict
- Can tip negatively too
- May be retrospective illusion

## Six Degrees of Separation

**Principle**: Any two people are connected by a chain of six or fewer acquaintances.

**When to Use**:
- Networking strategy
- Understanding social connectivity
- Information spread
- Influence mapping

**Implications**:
- World is highly connected
- Weak ties matter ("strength of weak ties")
- Information can spread quickly
- Small-world networks

**Example**: Milgram's letter experiment; LinkedIn connections; finding job through friend-of-friend.

**Pitfalls**:
- Actual number varies by context
- Not all paths equally accessible
- Social distance ≠ physical distance

## Bystander Effect

**Principle**: Individuals less likely to help when others are present (diffusion of responsibility).

**When to Use**:
- Emergency response design
- Organizational responsibility
- Understanding inaction
- Overcoming social inhibition

**Factors**:
- **Diffusion of responsibility**: Someone else will act
- **Social influence**: Others not acting suggests no help needed
- **Audience inhibition**: Fear of embarrassment

**Questions**:
- Who specifically is responsible?
- How can we make responsibility clear?
- How do we overcome bystander effect?

**Example**: More witnesses to emergency = less likely each will help. Corporate failures everyone saw but nobody stopped.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can be overcome with clear assignment
- Varies by cultural context
- Not universal in all situations

## Broken Windows Theory

**Principle**: Visible signs of disorder encourage further disorder and crime.

**When to Use**:
- Environmental design
- Organizational culture
- Quality control
- Setting standards

**Mechanism**:
- Small violations signal lack of care
- Leads to escalating violations
- Environment shapes behavior
- Order begets order

**Questions**:
- What "broken windows" exist?
- What message does environment send?
- How do we maintain order?

**Example**: Graffiti leads to more graffiti; messy code encourages more messy code; slack standards lead to slacker standards.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can justify over-policing
- Correlation not always causation
- Must balance with root causes

## Social Capital

**Principle**: Value derived from social networks, relationships, and community connections.

**When to Use**:
- Community development
- Career development
- Understanding inequality
- Building networks

**Types**:
- **Bonding**: Within-group connections (family, close friends)
- **Bridging**: Between-group connections (acquaintances, networks)
- **Linking**: Vertical connections (to power/resources)

**Questions**:
- What's my network strength?
- Do I have bonding and bridging capital?
- How can I build social capital?

**Example**: Job opportunities through networks; community support; business partnerships; political connections.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can exclude outsiders
- Requires maintenance
- Can be negative (crime networks)

---

## Cultural Capital

**Principle**: Non-financial assets (education, knowledge, skills, cultural familiarity) that provide social advantages.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding inequality
- Education strategy
- Social mobility
- Cultural navigation

**Forms**:
- **Embodied**: Internalized knowledge and skills
- **Objectified**: Cultural goods (books, art, credentials)
- **Institutionalized**: Formal recognition (degrees, certificates)

**Questions**:
- What cultural capital do I have?
- How is it valued in this context?
- How can it be acquired?

**Example**: University degree; knowing "right" references; speaking "right" way; familiarity with cultural norms.

**Pitfalls**:
- Perpetuates inequality
- Context-dependent value
- Can conflict between cultures

---

## Habitus

**Principle**: Deeply ingrained habits, skills, and dispositions acquired through life experiences.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding behavior patterns
- Cultural analysis
- Social reproduction
- Change resistance

**Characteristics**:
- Unconscious and automatic
- Shaped by social position
- Shapes perception and action
- Durable but not fixed

**Questions**:
- What unconscious assumptions guide behavior?
- How does background shape outlook?
- Can habitus change?

**Example**: Body language, taste preferences, risk tolerance, communication style shaped by upbringing and social class.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can seem deterministic
- Difficult to observe directly
- Resists conscious change

---

## Stigma

**Principle**: Social disapproval or discrimination based on perceived attributes.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding marginalization
- Public health campaigns
- Social justice
- Identity management

**Types**:
- **Physical**: Visible difference (disability)
- **Character**: Moral failing perception
- **Tribal**: Group membership (race, religion)

**Effects**:
- Social exclusion
- Internalized shame
- Discrimination
- Reduced opportunities

**Questions**:
- What stigmas exist?
- How do they affect behavior?
- How can stigma be reduced?

**Example**: Mental illness stigma preventing treatment; addiction seen as moral failure; criminal record limiting employment.

**Pitfalls**:
- Stigma reduction difficult
- Can backfire if poorly done
- Deeply embedded in culture

## Moral Panic

**Principle**: Widespread fear about threat to social order, often disproportionate to actual risk.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding media narratives
- Risk communication
- Political analysis
- Social movements

**Stages**:
1. Concern emerges
2. Hostility toward "deviants"
3. Consensus on threat
4. Disproportionality of response
5. Volatility (sudden appearance/disappearance)

**Questions**:
- Is response proportional to threat?
- Who benefits from the panic?
- What's the underlying anxiety?

**Example**: Satanic panic 1980s; video game violence; immigration waves; youth culture fears.

**Pitfalls**:
- Diverts from real issues
- Can cause harm to scapegoats
- May have kernel of truth

---

## Norm Cascades

**Principle**: Rapid spread of new social norms once critical threshold is crossed.

**When to Use**:
- Social change campaigns
- Understanding rapid shifts
- Tipping point analysis
- Cultural change

**Mechanism**:
- Early adopters face costs
- As more adopt, costs decrease
- Cascade accelerates
- New norm becomes established

**Questions**:
- What's the current norm?
- How close to cascade threshold?
- Who are norm entrepreneurs?

**Example**: Smoking bans spreading globally; #MeToo movement; environmental consciousness; mask-wearing during pandemic.

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to predict timing
- Can reverse direction
- May not consolidate
