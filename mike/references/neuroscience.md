# Neuroscience Models

Models for understanding brain function, behavior, and cognition at the neural level.

## Neuroplasticity

**Principle**: The brain's structure and function can change throughout life based on experience and learning.

**When to Use**:
- Habit formation/breaking
- Learning strategy
- Recovery from injury

**Questions**:
- What neural pathways am I strengthening?
- How long does this change take?
- What environment supports neuroplastic change?

**Example**: London taxi drivers develop larger hippocampi, musicians' motor cortex expansion, stroke recovery through rehabilitation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Change is slow (weeks/months, not days)
- Age reduces but doesn't eliminate plasticity
- Requires consistent practice

## Dopamine Reward System

**Principle**: Dopamine signals reward prediction, driving motivation and reinforcement learning.

**When to Use**:
- Habit design
- Motivation understanding
- Addiction awareness

**Questions**:
- What's triggering dopamine release?
- Is this intrinsic or extrinsic motivation?
- How can I leverage reward prediction?

**Example**: Variable reward schedules (slot machines), achievement badges, "just one more episode" streaming.

**Pitfalls**:
- Hijackable by supernormal stimuli
- Dopamine is about wanting, not liking
- Prediction error matters more than absolute reward

---

## Working Memory Capacity

**Principle**: Limited short-term storage (~4-7 items) for active information processing.

**When to Use**:
- Task design
- Information presentation
- Cognitive load management

**Questions**:
- How much am I trying to hold simultaneously?
- Can I chunk or offload some items?
- Is cognitive load overwhelming capacity?

**Example**: Phone numbers limited to 7 digits, multitasking failures, why writing things down helps.

**Pitfalls**:
- Individual variation (3-9 range)
- Complex items consume more capacity
- Stress further reduces capacity

---

## Attention Networks

**Principle**: Three distinct attention systems: alerting (arousal), orienting (selecting), executive (conflict resolution).

**When to Use**:
- Focus optimization
- Distraction management
- Task switching understanding

**Questions**:
- Which attention system is engaged?
- What's competing for attention?
- How can I reduce attentional demands?

**Example**: Alerting: coffee/urgency. Orienting: sudden movements. Executive: Stroop task, difficult decisions.

**Pitfalls**:
- Systems interfere with each other
- Attention is genuinely limited
- "Multitasking" is rapid switching

---

## Default Mode Network

**Principle**: Brain network active during rest, self-referential thought, and mind-wandering.

**When to Use**:
- Creativity cultivation
- Rest optimization
- Mental health understanding

**Questions**:
- When does my mind wander productively?
- Is this task-positive or default-mode activity?
- How can I balance focus and diffuse thinking?

**Example**: Shower thoughts, breakthroughs during walks, rumination in depression.

**Pitfalls**:
- Overactive DMN linked to anxiety/depression
- Suppression isn't always beneficial
- Balance with task-positive network needed

---

## Mirror Neurons

**Principle**: Neurons that fire both when acting and when observing others act - basis of imitation and empathy.

**When to Use**:
- Learning by observation
- Empathy development
- Social cognition

**Questions**:
- Can I learn by watching experts?
- How does observation shape behavior?
- What's the role in social understanding?

**Example**: Yawning contagion, learning sports by watching, feeling others' pain.

**Pitfalls**:
- Role sometimes overstated
- Not sufficient for complex skills
- Passive observation ≠ active practice

---

## Executive Function

**Principle**: High-level cognitive control for planning, decision-making, impulse control, and goal-directed behavior.

**When to Use**:
- Self-regulation
- Long-term planning
- Delayed gratification

**Questions**:
- What demands executive function?
- Is my executive function depleted?
- How can I support executive capacity?

**Example**: Resisting temptation, strategic planning, emotion regulation, task switching.

**Pitfalls**:
- Highly effortful and limited
- Depletes with use (ego depletion debate)
- Varies by time of day and stress

---

## Emotional Regulation

**Principle**: Neural mechanisms for monitoring and modifying emotional experiences and expressions.

**When to Use**:
- Stress management
- Decision-making under emotion
- Interpersonal effectiveness

**Questions**:
- What strategy am I using to regulate?
- Is this reappraisal or suppression?
- How effective is this regulation?

**Example**: Cognitive reappraisal (reframing), mindfulness, expressive suppression.

**Pitfalls**:
- Suppression often backfires
- Some situations need emotional expression
- Over-regulation reduces authenticity

---

## Stress Response

**Principle**: Acute stress activates fight-or-flight; chronic stress damages health and cognition.

**When to Use**:
- Performance optimization
- Health management
- Recovery planning

**Questions**:
- Is this acute or chronic stress?
- What's my stress recovery pattern?
- How can I leverage eustress vs avoiding distress?

**Example**: Exam anxiety (acute), burnout (chronic), exercise stress (beneficial).

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all stress is bad (hormesis)
- Individual stress thresholds vary
- Recovery matters as much as stressor

---

## Sleep Consolidation

**Principle**: Sleep is critical for memory consolidation, emotional processing, and cognitive function.

**When to Use**:
- Learning optimization
- Performance enhancement
- Health maintenance

**Questions**:
- Am I getting adequate sleep quality/quantity?
- What's the role of sleep in this learning?
- How does sleep deprivation affect performance?

**Example**: Memory consolidation during REM, problem-solving after sleep, cognitive decline with deprivation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Individual sleep needs vary
- Quality matters as much as quantity
- Timing (circadian rhythm) is critical
