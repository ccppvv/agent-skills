# Medicine & Health Models

Models from medical science and public health for understanding disease, treatment, and wellness.

## Dose-Response Relationship

**Principle**: The magnitude of response (therapeutic or toxic) depends on the dose administered; there's often a threshold and saturation point.

**When to Use**:
- Medication management
- Risk assessment
- Intervention design
- Resource allocation

**Questions**:
- What's the minimum effective dose?
- At what point do benefits plateau?
- When do side effects outweigh benefits?

**Example**: More medicine isn't always better; too little caffeine = no effect, optimal = alertness, too much = jitters/toxicity.

**Pitfalls**:
- Individual variation in response
- Hormesis (low dose beneficial, high dose harmful)
- Non-linear relationships

## Placebo Effect

**Principle**: Patient expectations and beliefs can produce real physiological changes and symptom relief, even with inert treatments.

**When to Use**:
- Clinical trial design
- Treatment protocols
- Doctor-patient communication
- Understanding subjective symptoms

**Questions**:
- How much is expectation vs actual treatment?
- Are we controlling for placebo in testing?
- How do we harness positive expectations ethically?

**Example**: Sugar pills reducing pain when patients believe they're painkillers; brand-name vs generic drug perception.

**Pitfalls**:
- Doesn't mean symptoms are "fake"
- Ethical issues in deception
- Effect size varies by condition

## Herd Immunity

**Principle**: When enough of a population is immune, disease spread is blocked, protecting even non-immune individuals.

**When to Use**:
- Vaccination strategy
- Public health policy
- Epidemic modeling
- Risk assessment

**Questions**:
- What's the threshold for this disease?
- Who are the vulnerable populations?
- How do we achieve sufficient coverage?

**Example**: ~95% measles vaccination protects infants too young to vaccinate; COVID-19 herd immunity debates.

**Pitfalls**:
- Threshold varies by disease (R0)
- Immunity can wane over time
- Assumes random mixing (not realistic)

## Epidemiology

**Principle**: Study of disease patterns, causes, and effects in populations to inform prevention and control strategies.

**When to Use**:
- Disease outbreak response
- Public health planning
- Risk factor identification
- Policy making

**Questions**:
- What's the attack rate and transmission pattern?
- What are the risk factors?
- How do we break the transmission chain?

**Example**: Contact tracing, case-control studies, identifying smoking as lung cancer cause.

**Pitfalls**:
- Correlation vs causation
- Confounding variables
- Population vs individual risk

---

## Preventive Medicine

**Principle**: Prevention is more effective and cost-efficient than treatment; intervene before disease develops.

**When to Use**:
- Health policy
- Resource allocation
- Lifestyle counseling
- Screening programs

**Questions**:
- What's preventable vs inevitable?
- What's the number needed to treat/prevent?
- Are we addressing root causes?

**Example**: Vaccines vs treating infections; exercise/diet vs treating diabetes; seatbelts vs trauma care.

**Pitfalls**:
- Prevention is less visible than treatment
- Long time horizons reduce urgency
- May over-medicalize healthy people

---

## Triage

**Principle**: Allocate scarce medical resources based on urgency and likelihood of benefit, not first-come-first-served.

**When to Use**:
- Emergency response
- Resource constraints
- Crisis management
- Priority setting

**Questions**:
- Who needs immediate attention?
- Who can wait safely?
- How do I maximize lives saved/outcomes?

**Example**: ER prioritization, mass casualty events, ICU bed allocation, organ transplant lists.

**Pitfalls**:
- Difficult ethical decisions
- Can feel unfair to those waiting
- Requires rapid assessment skills

---

## Differential Diagnosis

**Principle**: Generate and systematically evaluate multiple possible explanations for symptoms, ruling out alternatives.

**When to Use**:
- Medical diagnosis
- Problem solving with ambiguous symptoms
- Root cause analysis
- Avoiding premature conclusion

**Questions**:
- What else could this be?
- What would rule out each possibility?
- What's the most dangerous possibility to miss?

**Example**: Chest pain could be heart attack, anxiety, indigestion, muscle strain - test to differentiate.

**Pitfalls**:
- Cognitive biases (anchoring, availability)
- Rare diseases are rare (common things are common)
- Can lead to over-testing

## Risk Factors vs Causation

**Principle**: Association doesn't prove causation; risk factors increase probability but may not directly cause disease.

**When to Use**:
- Health research interpretation
- Prevention strategy
- Causal reasoning
- Evidence evaluation

**Questions**:
- Is this correlation or causation?
- What's the mechanism?
- Are there confounding variables?

**Example**: Smoking → lung cancer (causal); ice cream sales → drowning (confounded by summer weather).

**Pitfalls**:
- Bradford Hill criteria are guidelines, not proof
- Multiple risk factors interact
- Media often conflates association and causation

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Systems Thinking → Health as complex system
- Statistics → Epidemiological analysis
- Cost-Benefit → Healthcare resource allocation
- Network Effects → Disease transmission

**Conflicts with**:
- Individual variation (population averages)
- Deterministic thinking (probabilistic nature)
- Quick fixes (complex health outcomes)
