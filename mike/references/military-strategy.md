# Military Strategy Models

Strategic and tactical principles from military science applicable to business and life.

## Art of War (Sun Tzu Principles)

**Principle**: Know yourself and your enemy; win without fighting through superior positioning, deception, and resource management.

**When to Use**:
- Competitive strategy
- Negotiation
- Resource allocation
- Strategic positioning

**Questions**:
- Where can I win without fighting?
- What are the enemy's (competitor's) weaknesses?
- How do I achieve surprise or strategic advantage?

**Example**: "All warfare is based on deception"; attack where unexpected; make strong appear weak and weak appear strong.

**Pitfalls**:
- Ancient context may not transfer
- Can encourage excessive competition
- Deception has ethical limits

---

## Fog of War

**Principle**: Uncertainty, incomplete information, and chaos characterize real conflict; perfect information is impossible.

**When to Use**:
- Decision making under uncertainty
- Crisis response
- Accepting ambiguity
- Planning with imperfect information

**Questions**:
- What don't I know?
- How do I act despite uncertainty?
- What signals cut through the fog?

**Example**: Military commanders lacking real-time battlefield info; CEOs making decisions with partial market data.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can become excuse for inaction
- May miss available information
- Uncertainty varies in degree

---

## Force Multiplier

**Principle**: Elements that dramatically increase the effectiveness of a force without proportionally increasing size (technology, morale, training, terrain).

**When to Use**:
- Resource optimization
- Competitive advantage
- Leverage identification
- Strategic investment

**Questions**:
- What gives us disproportionate impact?
- Where can small input yield large output?
- What's our unique multiplier?

**Example**: Air superiority in war; automation in business; reputation in personal brand; network effects in platforms.

**Pitfalls**:
- Multipliers can be countered
- May create dependencies
- Not all claimed multipliers actually multiply

---

## Asymmetric Warfare

**Principle**: Weaker parties can prevail by using unconventional tactics that exploit stronger opponent's vulnerabilities and constraints.

**When to Use**:
- Underdog strategy
- Disruptive innovation
- Resource constraints
- Non-traditional competition

**Questions**:
- What rules favor the incumbent?
- Where is the strong opponent weakest?
- What can I do that they can't or won't?

**Example**: Guerrilla tactics vs conventional army; startups vs incumbents; mobile-first vs desktop-legacy.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can cross ethical lines
- May provoke overwhelming response
- Success breeds imitation (advantage erodes)

---

## Attrition Warfare

**Principle**: Win through gradual depletion of opponent's resources, morale, and capability until they can no longer continue.

**When to Use**:
- Resource advantage exploitation
- Long-term competition
- Endurance contests
- Grinding down opposition

**Questions**:
- Who has deeper resources?
- Can I outlast the opponent?
- What's the rate of attrition?

**Example**: WWI trench warfare; price wars (Walmart); endurance sports; extended negotiations.

**Pitfalls**:
- Costly for both sides
- Assumes resource advantage
- May destroy value for everyone

---

## Center of Gravity

**Principle**: The source of strength that provides cohesion, freedom of action, and will to fight; attacking it causes collapse.

**When to Use**:
- Strategic targeting
- Competitive analysis
- Problem solving
- Leverage point identification

**Questions**:
- What holds the system together?
- What, if removed, causes collapse?
- Where is the critical dependency?

**Example**: Supply lines in war; key talent in organizations; core technology in products; network effects in platforms.

**Pitfalls**:
- May be hard to identify
- Well-protected (by definition)
- Can have multiple centers

---

## Maneuver Warfare

**Principle**: Win through speed, surprise, and disruption rather than direct confrontation; paralyze decision-making and shatter cohesion.

**When to Use**:
- Speed advantage
- Avoiding strengths
- Creating confusion
- Rapid market changes

**Questions**:
- How can I move faster than opponent can respond?
- Where can I create maximum disruption?
- How do I target decision-making, not just assets?

**Example**: Blitzkrieg; agile startups vs slow corporates; fast iteration; getting inside OODA loop.

**Pitfalls**:
- Requires coordination at speed
- Can overextend
- Logistics become critical

---

## Defensive Advantage

**Principle**: Defense is easier than offense; defenders choose terrain, prepare positions, and force attackers to expose themselves.

**When to Use**:
- Protecting position
- Resource conservation
- Risk management
- Market leadership defense

**Questions**:
- What makes my position defensible?
- How do I raise costs of attack?
- What moats protect me?

**Example**: Fortifications, high ground; incumbent advantages (brand, distribution, data); regulatory moats; switching costs.

**Pitfalls**:
- Static defense can be outflanked
- Overinvestment in defense
- May become complacent

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Game Theory → Strategic interaction
- Systems Thinking → Complex operations
- OODA Loop → Decision speed
- Network Effects → Force multiplication

**Conflicts with**:
- Purely cooperative models
- Perfect information assumptions
- Ethical constraints on deception
