# Physics & Chemistry Models

Mental models from physical sciences.

## Inertia

**Principle**: Objects resist changes to their state of motion.

**Applications**: Organizational change, habit formation, momentum.

**Example**: Large organizations resist change; habits hard to break; projects continue despite obsolescence.

---

## Momentum

**Principle**: Mass in motion tends to stay in motion.

**Applications**: Business momentum, trends, compound effects.

**Example**: Growth begets growth; success breeds success; virtuous cycles.

---

## Velocity vs. Speed

**Principle**: Velocity includes direction; speed is magnitude only.

**Applications**: Strategy clarity, goal alignment, purposeful action.

**Example**: Busy vs. productive; activity vs. progress; fast in wrong direction.

---

## Leverage (Physical)

**Principle**: Small force amplified through mechanical advantage.

**Applications**: Force multiplication, efficiency, strategic positioning.

**Example**: Tools extending capability; platforms amplifying reach; strategic partnerships.

---

## Equilibrium

**Principle**: State where opposing forces balance.

**Applications**: Market equilibrium, work-life balance, system stability.

**Example**: Supply-demand balance; homeostasis; stable organizations.

---

## Potential vs. Kinetic Energy

**Principle**: Stored energy vs. energy in motion.

**Applications**: Capability vs. execution, talent vs. performance.

**Example**: Skills not applied; resources not deployed; potential未realized.

---

## Entropy (Thermodynamics)

**Principle**: Systems naturally tend toward disorder without energy input.

**Applications**: Organizational decay, knowledge loss, system maintenance.

**Example**: Code rot; skill atrophy; relationships need maintenance.

---

## Conservation Laws

**Principle**: Total quantity of certain properties remains constant.

**Applications**: Zero-sum games, resource allocation, trade-offs.

**Example**: Fixed time/energy budget; conservation of difficulty; no free lunch.

---

## Catalysis

**Principle**: Substance enabling reaction without being consumed.

**Applications**: Enabling factors, facilitators, infrastructure.

**Example**: Platforms enabling transactions; mentors enabling growth; tools enabling creation.

---

## Phase Transitions

**Principle**: Abrupt changes between states at critical thresholds.

**Applications**: Tipping points, discontinuous change, regime shifts.

**Example**: Water to ice; market crashes; viral adoption; organizational transformation.

---

## Resonance

**Principle**: System amplification at natural frequency.

**Applications**: Timing, natural fit, amplification.

**Example**: Message-market fit; cultural resonance; zeitgeist capture.

---

## Friction

**Principle**: Resistance to relative motion between surfaces.

**Applications**: Transaction costs, barriers, inefficiencies.

**Example**: Bureaucracy; switching costs; usability problems.

---

## Viscosity

**Principle**: Resistance to flow.

**Applications**: Organizational flow, information flow, process efficiency.

**Example**: Slow decision-making; information bottlenecks; rigid processes.

---

## Critical Mass (Nuclear)

**Principle**: Minimum mass for self-sustaining reaction.

**Applications**: Network effects, viral growth, minimum viable.

**Example**: Social network critical mass; nuclear reaction; epidemic threshold.

---

## Half-Life

**Principle**: Time for half of substance to decay.

**Applications**: Knowledge decay, relevance decay, trend lifespan.

**Example**: Skills becoming obsolete; information half-life; trend decay.

---

## Activation Energy (Chemical)

**Principle**: Minimum energy to initiate reaction.

**Applications**: Starting difficulty, initial barriers, change resistance.

**Example**: Project start-up costs; habit formation difficulty; overcoming inertia.

---

## Alloying

**Principle**: Mixing elements creates superior combined properties.

**Applications**: Combination benefits, synergy, hybrid approaches.

**Example**: Cross-functional teams; mixed strategies; hybrid technologies.

---

## Crystallization

**Principle**: Atoms arranging into ordered structure.

**Applications**: Organization formation, pattern emergence, structure creation.

**Example**: Organizational structure emerging; culture crystallizing; process formalization.

---

## Solubility

**Principle**: Maximum amount that can dissolve in solution.

**Applications**: Integration capacity, absorption limits, saturation.

**Example**: Maximum change an organization can absorb; information overload; attention limits.

---

## Surface Area to Volume Ratio

**Principle**: Smaller objects have higher ratio, affecting properties.

**Applications**: Organization size effects, scaling challenges.

**Example**: Small companies more agile; communication overhead scales; bureaucracy with size.

---

## Resonance

**Principle**: System vibrates with maximum amplitude when driven at its natural frequency; energy accumulates through frequency matching.

**When to Use**:
- Timing optimization
- Amplification strategies
- Understanding momentum
- Synergy creation

**Questions**:
- What's the natural frequency here?
- Are we pushing at the right rhythm?
- How do small inputs create large effects?

**Example**: Pushing swing at right intervals; marketing campaigns timed to cultural moments; compound habits; viral content matching zeitgeist.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can cause destructive oscillations
- Resonance changes as system changes
- May require precise timing

---

## Relativity (Physics Principle)

**Principle**: Observations depend on the observer's frame of reference; no absolute frame exists; motion is relative.

**When to Use**:
- Perspective-taking
- Understanding disagreements
- Context-dependent analysis
- Avoiding absolutism

**Questions**:
- From what perspective is this measured?
- How does changing reference frame change understanding?
- What's relative vs absolute here?

**Example**: Moving vs stationary depends on observer; time dilation at high speeds; organizational "fast" vs "slow" depends on comparison.

**Pitfalls**:
- Some things are invariant (speed of light)
- Can become pure relativism
- Not all perspectives are equally valid

---

## Wave-Particle Duality

**Principle**: Light (and matter) exhibits both wave-like and particle-like properties depending on how it's observed.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding multiple aspects
- Context-dependent behavior
- Complementary perspectives
- Measurement effects

**Questions**:
- What aspect do I observe in this context?
- Is this a wave or particle question?
- How does observation change the phenomenon?

**Example**: Employee as individual (particle) vs team member (wave); product features (particle) vs user experience (wave); data points vs trends.

**Pitfalls**:
- Analogy can be stretched too far
- Quantum weirdness doesn't scale to macro
- May obscure rather than clarify

---

## Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

**Principle**: Transfer of electrons between substances; one oxidized (loses electrons), one reduced (gains electrons); coupled reactions.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding exchanges
- Trade-offs
- Value transfers
- Coupled processes

**Questions**:
- What's being exchanged?
- Who gains and who loses?
- Are these processes coupled?

**Example**: Mentor-mentee (experience for fresh perspective); buyer-seller (money for goods); debt (present consumption for future payment).

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all exchanges are zero-sum
- Can miss non-material exchanges
- Oxidation without reduction doesn't occur

---

## Chemical Bonds

**Principle**: Atoms connect through shared or transferred electrons; bond strength determines stability and required energy to break.

**When to Use**:
- Relationship strength assessment
- Connection analysis
- Stability evaluation
- Required effort estimation

**Questions**:
- What holds this together?
- How strong is this bond?
- What energy breaks this connection?

**Example**: Strong team bonds (hard to break up); weak customer loyalty (easy to switch); covalent partnerships (shared resources) vs ionic (exchange-based).

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all bonds are equal
- Context affects bond strength
- Can anthropomorphize too much

---

## Solubility

**Principle**: Maximum amount that can dissolve in solution; "like dissolves like" (polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar).

**When to Use**:
- Integration capacity
- Compatibility assessment
- Absorption limits
- Cultural fit

**Questions**:
- How much can be absorbed?
- Are these compatible?
- What's the saturation point?

**Example**: New hires absorbed into culture; change management capacity; information overload; "oil and water don't mix."

**Pitfalls**:
- Temperature/pressure affect solubility
- Forced mixing can create emulsions (unstable)
- Saturation isn't always obvious

---

## Catalysts

**Principle**: Substances that speed up reactions without being consumed; lower activation energy required.

**When to Use**:
- Identifying enablers
- Reducing barriers
- Acceleration strategies
- Facilitator roles

**Questions**:
- What could catalyze this change?
- What lowers the activation barrier?
- Who/what enables without being consumed?

**Example**: Facilitator in meetings; seed funding for startups; mentorship accelerating growth; regulatory changes enabling innovation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Catalysts don't change equilibrium (just speed)
- Can catalyze unwanted reactions too
- May need to be very specific
