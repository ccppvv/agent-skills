# Economic Thinking Models

Understanding incentives, trade-offs, and resource allocation.

## Supply and Demand

**Principle**: Prices emerge from intersection of supply (willingness to sell) and demand (willingness to buy).

**When to Use**:
- Understanding price changes
- Market analysis
- Resource allocation

**Key Concepts**:
- **Equilibrium**: Price where quantity supplied = quantity demanded
- **Shifts**: Changes in supply/demand curves (vs. movement along curve)
- **Elasticity**: Responsiveness to price changes

**Questions**:
- What's affecting supply? Demand?
- How elastic is demand/supply?
- What's the new equilibrium?

---

## Marginal Thinking

**Principle**: Decisions based on incremental changes, not totals.

**Types**:
- **Marginal Benefit**: Additional benefit from one more unit
- **Marginal Cost**: Additional cost from one more unit
- **Optimal point**: Where marginal benefit = marginal cost

**When to Use**:
- Optimization decisions
- Pricing strategy
- Resource allocation

**Questions**:
- What's the benefit/cost of one more?
- Where does marginal benefit = marginal cost?
- Am I thinking in averages when I should think in margins?

**Examples**:
- Should I study one more hour? (marginal benefit vs. cost)
- Price one more feature? (marginal revenue vs. cost)
- Hire one more person? (marginal productivity vs. salary)

## Comparative Advantage

**Principle**: Specialize in activities where you have the lowest opportunity cost, even if not absolute best.

**vs. Absolute Advantage**: Being best at everything doesn't mean you should do everything.

**When to Use**:
- Division of labor
- Trade decisions
- Team composition

**Example**:
- Doctor could be best lawyer and doctor
- But if opportunity cost of lawyering is forgone surgeries
- Should specialize in surgery and trade for legal services

**Questions**:
- What's my relative efficiency?
- What's my opportunity cost?
- Where should I specialize?

## Incentives

**Principle**: People respond to incentives. Often in unexpected ways.

**Types**:
- **Economic**: Money, prices, costs
- **Social**: Status, reputation, belonging
- **Moral**: Values, ethics, identity

**When to Use**:
- Designing systems
- Predicting behavior
- Diagnosing problems

**Questions**:
- What are the incentives?
- What behavior do they actually encourage?
- What unintended incentives exist?

**Examples**:
- Cobra effect: Bounty for dead cobras → People breed cobras
- Goodhart's Law: Measure becomes target → Ceases to be good measure
- Soviet nail factory: Measured by weight → Huge, useless nails

## Moral Hazard

**Principle**: Separation of risk-taking from consequences leads to excessive risk.

**When to Use**:
- Insurance design
- Contract structure
- Organizational design

**Questions**:
- Who bears the risk? Who makes decisions?
- How aligned are incentives?
- What happens if things go wrong?

**Examples**:
- Bank bailouts → Riskier lending
- Insurance → Less careful behavior
- Performance guarantees → Cutting corners

**Solutions**:
- Skin in the game
- Monitoring and oversight
- Align incentives with consequences

---

## Adverse Selection

**Principle**: Information asymmetry causes high-risk participants to disproportionately select into markets.

**When to Use**:
- Market design
- Pricing strategy
- Understanding market failures

**Examples**:
- Used car market: Sellers know quality, buyers don't → Only lemons sold
- Insurance: High-risk people more likely to buy → Premiums rise → Low-risk exit
- Employees: People quitting know more than employers → Retention challenges

**Solutions**:
- Signaling (credentials, warranties)
- Screening (testing, requirements)
- Pooling (mandatory participation)

---

## Economies of Scale

**Principle**: Unit costs decrease as production volume increases.

**Sources**:
- Fixed cost spreading
- Specialization
- Volume discounts
- Learning curves

**When to Use**:
- Business model analysis
- Competitive strategy
- Make vs. buy decisions

**Questions**:
- What are fixed vs. variable costs?
- How does scale affect unit economics?
- What's minimum efficient scale?

**Pitfalls**:
- Diseconomies of scale (coordination costs)
- Diminishing returns
- Strategic vulnerabilities (high fixed costs)

---

## Network Effects

**Principle**: Product/service value increases with number of users.

**Types**:
- **Direct**: More users = more value (phones, social networks)
- **Indirect**: More users → more complements (platforms, marketplaces)
- **Cross-side**: Different user groups benefit each other (marketplaces)

**When to Use**:
- Business model evaluation
- Competitive analysis
- Growth strategy

**Characteristics**:
- Winner-take-all dynamics
- High switching costs
- Defensible moats
- Cold start problem

**Examples**:
- Social media
- Marketplaces
- Payment networks
- Operating systems

---

## Principal-Agent Problem

**Principle**: Conflicts arise when one party (agent) makes decisions on behalf of another (principal) with misaligned incentives.

**When to Use**:
- Organizational design
- Contract structure
- Understanding dysfunction

**Examples**:
- Shareholders (principal) vs. CEOs (agent)
- Patients (principal) vs. Doctors (agent)
- Voters (principal) vs. Politicians (agent)

**Solutions**:
- Monitoring and oversight
- Incentive alignment
- Skin in the game
- Reputation mechanisms
