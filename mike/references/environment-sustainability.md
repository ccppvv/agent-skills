# Environment & Sustainability Models

Models for understanding ecological systems, resource management, and long-term sustainability.

## Carrying Capacity

**Principle**: Maximum population size an environment can sustain indefinitely given resources, waste assimilation, and other constraints.

**When to Use**:
- Resource planning
- Growth limits assessment
- Sustainability analysis
- System capacity evaluation

**Questions**:
- What's the sustainable limit?
- Are we approaching or exceeding capacity?
- What resources constrain capacity?

**Example**: Deer population in forest limited by food; server capacity for users; Earth's human population limits.

**Pitfalls**:
- Capacity isn't fixed (technology changes it)
- Multiple limiting factors
- May increase then crash suddenly

## Circular Economy

**Principle**: Design systems where waste becomes input; eliminate waste through reuse, refurbishment, remanufacturing, and recycling.

**When to Use**:
- Product design
- Business model innovation
- Waste reduction
- Resource efficiency

**Questions**:
- Where does waste go?
- Can outputs become inputs?
- How do we close the loop?

**Example**: Industrial symbiosis (one factory's waste = another's input); product-as-service models; composting; upcycling.

**Pitfalls**:
- Some losses are thermodynamically inevitable
- May be more expensive initially
- Requires system coordination

---

## Carbon Footprint / Life Cycle Assessment

**Principle**: Total greenhouse gas emissions (or other impacts) throughout a product's entire life cycle from extraction to disposal.

**When to Use**:
- Environmental impact assessment
- Comparative evaluation
- Decision making
- Sustainability reporting

**Questions**:
- What's the full life cycle impact?
- Where are the major emissions/impacts?
- How do alternatives compare?

**Example**: Electric car vs gas car (must include manufacturing + electricity source); local vs imported food; paper vs plastic bags.

**Pitfalls**:
- Boundary definition matters
- Data availability/quality issues
- Can be gamed by narrow boundaries

---

## Renewable Resources

**Principle**: Resources that regenerate naturally if harvested at or below regeneration rate; exceed regeneration and they become depleted.

**When to Use**:
- Resource management
- Sustainability planning
- Investment decisions
- Policy design

**Questions**:
- What's the regeneration rate?
- Are we harvesting sustainably?
- What maintains the resource base?

**Example**: Forests, fisheries, groundwater; sustainable forestry vs clear-cutting; fishing quotas to prevent collapse.

**Pitfalls**:
- Regeneration depends on healthy systems
- Can tip into non-renewable if degraded
- Tragedy of the commons

---

## Biodiversity

**Principle**: Variety of life forms provides ecosystem resilience, services, and options; loss reduces function and adaptability.

**When to Use**:
- Conservation planning
- Risk management
- Resilience building
- Option preservation

**Questions**:
- What diversity exists?
- What's lost if we lose species X?
- How do we maintain diversity?

**Example**: Diverse crops resist pests better; genetic diversity enables adaptation; diverse investments reduce portfolio risk.

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to value until lost
- Keystone species have outsized importance
- Can justify preserving harmful diversity

---

## Externality

**Principle**: Costs or benefits not reflected in market prices; negative externalities impose costs on others; positive externalities benefit others.

**When to Use**:
- Market failure analysis
- Policy design
- Cost-benefit analysis
- Responsibility assignment

**Questions**:
- Who bears costs not in the price?
- What benefits aren't captured?
- How do we internalize externalities?

**Example**: Pollution (negative), education (positive), carbon pricing, noise, traffic congestion, vaccines (herd immunity).

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to quantify
- Who compensates whom?
- Can justify excessive intervention

---

## Ecosystem Services

**Principle**: Benefits humans derive from ecosystems (provisioning, regulating, cultural, supporting); often unpriced but valuable.

**When to Use**:
- Environmental valuation
- Development impact assessment
- Conservation justification
- Policy analysis

**Questions**:
- What services does this ecosystem provide?
- What's the value if lost?
- Can we replace or restore it?

**Example**: Wetlands filter water, forests sequester carbon, bees pollinate crops, oceans regulate climate.

**Pitfalls**:
- Commodification concerns
- Valuation is difficult and contested
- May miss intrinsic value

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Systems Thinking → Complex ecological interactions
- Tragedy of Commons → Resource management
- Long-term Thinking → Intergenerational effects
- Externalities + Public Goods → Market failures

**Conflicts with**:
- Short-term profit maximization
- Infinite growth assumptions
- Narrow accounting boundaries
