# Deep Psychology Models

## Growth vs. Fixed Mindset
**Principle**: Abilities can be developed vs. innately fixed.
**Application**: Learning; development; feedback reception.
**Example**: "I can't yet" vs. "I can't"; embracing challenges; viewing failure as learning.

## Cognitive Dissonance
**Principle**: Mental discomfort from conflicting beliefs/behaviors.
**Application**: Behavior change; understanding rationalization; persuasion.
**Example**: Smoker knowing health risks; justifying poor decisions; post-purchase rationalization.

## Learned Helplessness
**Principle**: Repeated failure leads to giving up even when success possible.
**Application**: Motivation; resilience; depression understanding.
**Example**: Giving up after failures; "nothing I do matters"; breaking victim mentality.

## Impostor Syndrome
**Principle**: Despite success, feeling like a fraud who doesn't deserve it.
**Application**: Self-awareness; career development; confidence building.
**Example**: High achievers doubting abilities; attributing success to luck.

## Maslow's Hierarchy  
**Principle**: Needs arranged in pyramid (physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualization).
**Application**: Motivation; prioritization; human needs understanding.
**Example**: Can't focus on growth when survival threatened; hierarchy of concerns.

## Flow State (Csiks Zentmihalyi)
**Principle**: Optimal experience when challenge matches skill; total absorption.
**Application**: Performance; motivation; experience design.
**Example**: Athletes "in the zone"; programmers losing track of time; optimal difficulty.

## Default Mode Network
**Principle**: Brain's baseline state; mind-wandering, self-reflection.
**Application**: Creativity; rest importance; attention management.
**Example**: Shower thoughts; insights during rest; importance of downtime.

## Spotlight Effect
**Principle**: Overestimating how much others notice about us.
**Application**: Social anxiety reduction; perspective; self-consciousness.
**Example**: Nobody cares about your small mistakes as much as you think.

## Reactance Theory
**Principle**: When freedom threatened, motivated to restore it.
**Application**: Persuasion; reverse psychology; change resistance.
**Example**: Telling someone "don't" makes them want to; censorship backfiring.

## Cognitive Load Theory
**Principle**: Limited working memory capacity; manage information load.
**Application**: Learning; communication; UX design.
**Example**: Chunking information; progressive disclosure; avoiding overwhelm.

## Parkinson's Law of Triviality (Bikeshedding)
**Principle**: Time spent on topic inversely proportional to importance.
**Application**: Meeting management; focus; priority setting.
**Example**: Hours on trivial decisions; avoiding hard problems; bikeshed color debates.

## Mere-Exposure Effect
**Principle**: Familiarity breeds liking.
**Application**: Marketing; relationship building; brand building.
**Example**: Repeated exposure increases preference; familiarity = safety.

## Peak-End Rule
**Principle**: Experiences judged by peak and end, not average.
**Application**: Experience design; customer service; memory.
**Example**: End experience matters most; design for strong finish.

## Negativity Bias
**Principle**: Negative experiences more impactful than positive.
**Application**: Risk management; communication; relationship maintenance.
**Example**: One criticism overshadows ten compliments; bad news sticks.

## Zeigarnik Effect  
**Principle**: Unfinished tasks more memorable than completed ones.
**Application**: Productivity; motivation; attention.
**Example**: Cliffhangers work; open loops demand closure; to-do lists create tension.

## Cognitive Ease vs. Strain
**Principle**: Easy-to-process = feels true; difficult = feels false.
**Application**: Communication; persuasion; design.
**Example**: Simple fonts more believable; clear writing more persuasive.

## Affect Heuristic
**Principle**: Emotions guide judgments more than logic.
**Application**: Decision-making; persuasion; understanding choices.
**Example**: Like brand → judge quality high; emotional first, rational second.

## Construal Level Theory
**Principle**: Psychological distance affects thinking (concrete vs. abstract).
**Application**: Planning; communication; motivation.
**Example**: Near future = concrete details; far future = abstract goals.

## Self-Determination Theory
**Principle**: Autonomy, competence, relatedness drive intrinsic motivation.
**Application**: Motivation; management; engagement.
**Example**: Control, mastery, purpose; intrinsic > extrinsic motivation.

## Social Comparison Theory
**Principle**: Self-evaluation through comparison with others.
**Application**: Well-being; motivation; social media effects.
**Example**: Upward comparison (inspiring or demoralizing); downward comparison (reassuring).

## Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

**Principle**: Human needs arranged in pyramid: physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualization.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding motivation
- Product design
- Marketing
- Leadership
- Well-being assessment

**Levels** (bottom to top):
1. **Physiological**: Food, water, sleep, shelter
2. **Safety**: Security, stability, protection
3. **Love/Belonging**: Relationships, connection, community
4. **Esteem**: Recognition, achievement, respect
5. **Self-Actualization**: Purpose, growth, potential

**Questions**:
- Which needs are unmet?
- Am I focusing on right level?
- Are basic needs satisfied first?

**Example**: Can't focus on career (esteem) when worried about rent (safety). Employee engagement requires belonging first.

**Pitfalls**:
- Too rigid (people multitask needs)
- Cultural bias (collectivist cultures differ)
- Self-actualization vague
- Not always hierarchical

## Pavlovian Conditioning (Classical)

**Principle**: Neutral stimulus paired with natural stimulus creates learned response.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding automatic responses
- Brand associations
- Habit formation
- Phobia treatment

**Process**:
- Unconditioned stimulus (food) → Unconditioned response (salivation)
- + Neutral stimulus (bell)
- = Conditioned stimulus (bell) → Conditioned response (salivation)

**Examples**:
- Brand logos → positive feelings
- Workplace → stress response
- Music → memories/emotions
- Phobias developed through association

**Applications**:
- Marketing: Product + positive imagery
- Therapy: Desensitization
- Training: Associations

**Pitfalls**:
- Extinction occurs without reinforcement
- Can create unwanted associations
- Not all learning is associative

## Operant Conditioning

**Principle**: Behavior shaped by consequences (reinforcement and punishment).

**When to Use**:
- Behavior modification
- Training and education
- Game design
- Management

**Four Quadrants**:
- **Positive Reinforcement**: Add reward → increase behavior
- **Negative Reinforcement**: Remove aversive → increase behavior
- **Positive Punishment**: Add aversive → decrease behavior
- **Negative Punishment**: Remove reward → decrease behavior

**Schedules**:
- **Continuous**: Every time (fast learning, fast extinction)
- **Interval**: Time-based (salary)
- **Ratio**: Response-based (commission)
- **Variable**: Unpredictable (gambling, social media)

**Example**: Variable ratio (slot machines) most addictive; praise for good work; removing privileges for bad behavior.

**Pitfalls**:
- Over-reliance on extrinsic motivation
- Punishment often backfires
- Variable schedules can be exploitative

## Cognitive Load Theory

**Principle**: Working memory has limited capacity; learning requires managing cognitive load.

**When to Use**:
- Instructional design
- UX design
- Communication
- Training

**Three Types**:
- **Intrinsic**: Inherent complexity
- **Extraneous**: Poor design/presentation
- **Germane**: Processing that aids learning

**Strategies**:
- Reduce extraneous (clear design)
- Manage intrinsic (chunking, sequencing)
- Optimize germane (schemas, practice)

**Questions**:
- Am I overloading working memory?
- Can this be simplified?
- What's essential vs nice-to-have?

**Example**: Step-by-step instructions better than all-at-once; visual + verbal better than redundant; worked examples reduce load.

**Pitfalls**:
- Over-simplification
- Individual differences
- Expertise changes load

## Gestalt Principles

**Principle**: Perception organizes elements into unified wholes ("whole is greater than sum of parts").

**When to Use**:
- Visual design
- UX/UI
- Communication
- Problem-solving

**Key Principles**:
- **Proximity**: Close = grouped
- **Similarity**: Similar = grouped
- **Continuity**: Follow smooth paths
- **Closure**: Complete incomplete patterns
- **Figure-Ground**: Distinguish object from background
- **Common Fate**: Moving together = grouped

**Example**: 
- Logo design uses closure
- Forms use proximity to group fields
- White space separates sections
- Visual hierarchy

**Pitfalls**:
- Cultural differences
- Can be exploited (camouflage)
- Not universal

## Flow State

**Principle**: Optimal experience when challenge matches skill, creating complete immersion.

**When to Use**:
- Peak performance
- Engagement design
- Skill development
- Well-being

**Conditions**:
- Clear goals
- Immediate feedback
- Challenge-skill balance
- Deep concentration
- Loss of self-consciousness
- Time distortion

**Sweet Spot**:
- Too easy = boredom
- Too hard = anxiety
- Just right = flow

**Questions**:
- Is challenge appropriate for skill?
- Are goals clear?
- Is feedback immediate?
- What's preventing immersion?

**Example**: Athletes "in the zone"; deep coding sessions; musical performance; engaging games.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not sustainable all day
- Can lose perspective
- May neglect other needs

## Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

**Principle**: Belief about future influences behavior to make belief come true.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding expectations effects
- Leadership
- Education
- Performance management

**Mechanism**:
1. Form belief/expectation
2. Behavior changes accordingly
3. Others respond to behavior
4. Belief confirmed

**Examples**:
- **Pygmalion Effect**: High expectations → better performance
- **Golem Effect**: Low expectations → worse performance
- Teacher expectations affect student outcomes
- Stereotype threat

**Questions**:
- What are my expectations?
- Am I treating people according to expectations?
- How do expectations shape behavior?

**Pitfalls**:
- Can perpetuate biases
- Not all prophecies self-fulfill
- Individual agency still matters

## Priming

**Principle**: Exposure to stimulus influences response to subsequent stimulus.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding unconscious influence
- Marketing
- Behavioral design
- Decision-making

**Types**:
- **Semantic**: Word associations
- **Perceptual**: Sensory cues
- **Conceptual**: Abstract concepts
- **Affective**: Emotional states

**Examples**:
- Money imagery → selfish behavior
- Elderly words → walking slower
- Anchoring uses priming
- Store scents → purchases

**Questions**:
- What's priming my thinking?
- What environmental cues exist?
- How can I use/avoid priming?

**Pitfalls**:
- Replication crisis in priming research
- Effects often small
- Context-dependent
- Awareness may reduce effect

## Schemas

**Principle**: Mental frameworks organizing knowledge and guiding perception and interpretation.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding perception
- Learning design
- Expertise development
- Communication

**Functions**:
- Organize information
- Fill gaps (inference)
- Guide attention
- Enable efficiency

**Types**:
- Person schemas (stereotypes)
- Event schemas (scripts)
- Role schemas (expectations)
- Self-schemas (identity)

**Example**: Restaurant schema: enter → seat → menu → order → eat → pay → leave. Shapes expectations and behavior.

**Pitfalls**:
- Stereotyping
- Confirmation bias
- Resistance to schema-inconsistent info
- Hard to update

## Zeigarnik Effect

**Principle**: Incomplete tasks create cognitive tension, making them more memorable than completed ones.

**When to Use**:
- Productivity techniques
- Engagement design
- Memory enhancement
- Motivation

**Mechanism**:
- Open loops demand closure
- Tension drives memory
- Completion releases tension

**Applications**:
- **Cliffhangers**: TV shows, books
- **Progress bars**: Show incompleteness
- **To-do lists**: Creates tension
- **Checkpoints**: Games, learning

**Questions**:
- What open loops exist?
- Can I use tension productively?
- When to close loops for relief?

**Example**: Started task stays on mind; Netflix "Next episode" button; tutorial progress; game save points.

**Pitfalls**:
- Too many open loops = stress
- Anxiety if can't complete
- May prevent rest

## Beginner's Mind (Shoshin)

**Principle**: Approach situations with openness, eagerness, and lack of preconceptions (as a beginner would).

**When to Use**:
- Innovation
- Learning
- Problem-solving
- Overcoming expertise blindness

**Characteristics**:
- Openness to possibilities
- No fixed expectations
- Curious questioning
- Humility
- Fresh perspective

**Questions**:
- What am I assuming?
- How would a beginner see this?
- What "obvious" questions am I not asking?
- What if everything I know is wrong?

**Example**: Expert misses simple solution; beginner asks "why can't we just...?"; fresh eyes find obvious problems.

**Benefits**:
- Avoid confirmation bias
- Question assumptions
- See new possibilities
- Avoid expertise trap

**Pitfalls**:
- Can ignore valuable expertise
- May waste time re-learning
- Balance needed with mastery
