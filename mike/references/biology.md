# Biology & Evolution Models

Mental models from biology, genetics, and evolutionary theory.

## Natural Selection

**Principle**: Traits that increase survival/reproduction become more common over time.

**Applications**: Competition, adaptation, optimization, market dynamics.

**Example**: Better products survive market; effective strategies replicate in organizations.

---

## Sexual Selection

**Principle**: Traits that increase mating success, even if reducing survival.

**Applications**: Signaling, status competition, costly displays.

**Example**: Luxury goods as peacock tails; credentials as mating displays.

---

## Red Queen Effect

**Principle**: Continuous adaptation needed just to maintain relative position.

**Applications**: Competition, innovation, arms races.

**Example**: Security vs. hacking; brands vs. competitors; education inflation.

---

## Punctuated Equilibrium

**Principle**: Long stability periods interrupted by rapid change.

**Applications**: Organizational change, market disruption, technological shifts.

**Example**: Industry disruption after stability; sudden organizational transformation.

---

## Evolutionary Stable Strategy (ESS)

**Principle**: Strategy that, if adopted by population, cannot be invaded by alternative.

**Applications**: Game theory, competitive strategy, social norms.

**Example**: Cooperation in repeated games; pricing strategies; cultural practices.

---

## Adaptation

**Principle**: Organisms adjust to environment through selection or learning.

**Applications**: Organizational learning, market adaptation, skill development.

**Example**: Companies adapting to tech changes; personal skill evolution.

---

## Extinction

**Principle**: Inability to adapt leads to elimination.

**Applications**: Business failure, obsolescence, career changes.

**Example**: Kodak and digital; bookstores and Amazon; coal and renewables.

---

## Speciation

**Principle**: Populations diverge into distinct species when isolated.

**Applications**: Market segmentation, niche specialization, divergent evolution.

**Example**: Mobile vs. desktop; B2B vs. B2C; geographic market differences.

---

## Genetic Drift

**Principle**: Random changes in frequency of traits over time.

**Applications**: Understanding randomness in outcomes, luck vs. skill.

**Example**: Startup success variations; career path randomness.

---

## Fitness Landscape

**Principle**: Multiple peaks of fitness; local vs. global optima.

**Applications**: Optimization, innovation, strategy.

**Example**: Local optimization traps; need for exploration vs. exploitation.

---

## Symbiosis

**Principle**: Mutually beneficial relationships between different entities.

**Applications**: Partnerships, ecosystems, win-win strategies.

**Example**: Business partnerships; platform ecosystems; strategic alliances.

---

## Parasitism

**Principle**: One organism benefits at expense of another.

**Applications**: Understanding extractive relationships, rent-seeking.

**Example**: Regulatory capture; middleman extraction; toxic relationships.

---

## Ecosystem

**Principle**: Interconnected community of organisms and environment.

**Applications**: Business ecosystems, platform dynamics, stakeholder networks.

**Example**: Apple ecosystem; startup hubs; industry clusters.

---

## Niche

**Principle**: Specific role/position in ecosystem that organism fills.

**Applications**: Market positioning, specialization, competitive advantage.

**Example**: Business niche markets; career specialization; positioning.

---

## Carrying Capacity

**Principle**: Maximum population environment can sustain.

**Applications**: Market sizing, resource limits, sustainable growth.

**Example**: Market saturation; resource constraints; growth limits.

---

## Predator-Prey Dynamics

**Principle**: Cyclical population changes between predator and prey.

**Applications**: Market cycles, competition dynamics, boom-bust patterns.

**Example**: Startup funding cycles; hiring/firing patterns; inventory cycles.

---

## Mimicry

**Principle**: Organisms evolve to resemble others for advantage.

**Applications**: Imitation strategy, copying success, competitive mimicry.

**Example**: Fast followers; me-too products; cargo cult behaviors.

---

## Vestigial Structures

**Principle**: Features that lost original function but remain.

**Applications**: Legacy systems, organizational bloat, outdated practices.

**Example**: Appendix in code; outdated processes; legacy infrastructure.

---

## Co-evolution

**Principle**: Species evolve in response to each other.

**Applications**: Competitive dynamics, partner evolution, ecosystem development.

**Example**: Security and hackers; regulations and compliance; tech and society.

---

## Bottleneck Effect

**Principle**: Sharp population reduction reduces genetic diversity.

**Applications**: Crisis impacts, talent loss, knowledge loss.

**Example**: Layoffs losing institutional knowledge; market crashes eliminating players.

---

## Genetic Drift

**Principle**: Random changes in gene frequencies over time, especially in small populations; evolution by chance rather than selection.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding random effects
- Small sample analysis
- Accidental outcomes
- Path dependency

**Questions**:
- How much is random vs selected?
- Does population size amplify randomness?
- What's locked in by chance?

**Example**: Startup culture emerging randomly from first hires; QWERTY keyboard persisting despite better alternatives; small team norms becoming company culture.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can underestimate selection pressure
- May excuse poor outcomes as "luck"
- Randomness decreases with scale

---

## Mutation

**Principle**: Random genetic changes create variation; most harmful or neutral, rare beneficial ones enable evolution.

**When to Use**:
- Innovation strategy
- Experimentation
- Variation generation
- Long-term adaptation

**Questions**:
- What's the mutation rate?
- How do we increase useful mutations?
- What kills harmful mutations quickly?

**Example**: A/B testing creates mutations; startup pivots; random ideas; most fail but rare success drives evolution.

**Pitfalls**:
- Most mutations are harmful
- Requires selection mechanism
- Can't plan which mutations help
