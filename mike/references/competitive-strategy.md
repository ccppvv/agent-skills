# Competitive Strategy Models

Strategic frameworks for building and maintaining competitive advantage.

## Economic Moat

**Principle**: Sustainable competitive advantages that protect long-term profits (like moat protects castle).

**When to Use**:
- Investment analysis
- Strategy development
- Competitive analysis
- Business model design

**Five Moat Types** (Michael Porter + Buffett):
1. **Network Effects**: Value increases with users
2. **Switching Costs**: Expensive/painful to change
3. **Cost Advantages**: Structural cost leadership
4. **Intangible Assets**: Brands, patents, regulations
5. **Efficient Scale**: Natural monopoly in market

**Questions**:
- What protects our profits?
- How wide is the moat?
- Is it deepening or eroding?
- What could breach it?

**Example**: 
- Google: Network effects + switching costs
- Costco: Cost advantages + switching costs
- Coca-Cola: Brand (intangible asset)
- Utilities: Efficient scale

**Pitfalls**:
- Moats erode (Kodak, Blockbuster)
- Can create complacency
- Technology disrupts moats
- Regulation can breach moats

## Switching Costs

**Principle**: Costs (monetary, time, effort, risk) of changing from one product/service to another.

**When to Use**:
- Customer retention strategy
- Competitive positioning
- Pricing power analysis
- Lock-in strategy

**Types**:
- **Financial**: Termination fees, new setup costs
- **Procedural**: Time, effort to switch
- **Relational**: Lost relationships/history
- **Data**: Migration difficulty
- **Learning**: Need to learn new system

**Examples**:
- **High**: Enterprise software, bank accounts, Adobe ecosystem
- **Low**: Soap brands, ride-sharing apps, streaming services

**Questions**:
- What makes customers stay?
- How can we increase switching costs?
- Are we trapped by own switching costs?

**Pitfalls**:
- Can trap yourself too
- May reduce acquisition
- Customers resent artificial costs
- Can breed complacency

## First-Mover Advantage

**Principle**: Benefits from being first to market with innovation.

**When to Use**:
- Launch timing decisions
- Competitive strategy
- Innovation planning
- Resource allocation

**Advantages**:
- Brand recognition
- Customer lock-in
- Learning curve
- Resource preemption
- Technology leadership
- Network effects

**Disadvantages** (Fast Follower):
- Education costs
- Technology risk
- Market uncertainty
- Free rider problem

**Questions**:
- Is being first worth it here?
- Can we defend first-mover position?
- What if we're second but better?
- Fast follower advantages?

**Example**: 
- **Winners**: Amazon (e-commerce), Google (search), eBay (auctions)
- **Losers**: Friendster (social), Netscape (browser), Palm (PDA)

**Pitfalls**:
- Often overrated
- Second-mover can learn
- Execution > timing
- May be too early

## Strategic Positioning

**Principle**: Choosing unique activities to deliver distinct value vs. competitors.

**When to Use**:
- Strategy formulation
- Differentiation
- Market segmentation
- Competitive analysis

**Three Generic Strategies** (Porter):
1. **Cost Leadership**: Lowest cost producer
2. **Differentiation**: Unique value
3. **Focus**: Niche specialization

**Positioning Principle**:
- Strategy is choosing what NOT to do
- Trade-offs are essential
- Fit among activities creates advantage

**Questions**:
- How are we different?
- What trade-offs have we made?
- Why can't competitors copy us?
- Do our activities reinforce each other?

**Example**:
- Southwest: Low cost + point-to-point
- Whole Foods: Premium + organic focus
- IKEA: Low cost + self-assembly + modern design

**Pitfalls**:
- Stuck in the middle
- Imitation
- Straddling positions
- Losing distinctiveness

## Value Chain Analysis

**Principle**: Break down business into discrete activities to identify sources of competitive advantage.

**When to Use**:
- Cost analysis
- Differentiation opportunities
- Vertical integration decisions
- Outsourcing decisions

**Primary Activities**:
- Inbound logistics
- Operations
- Outbound logistics
- Marketing & sales
- Service

**Support Activities**:
- Firm infrastructure
- HR management
- Technology development
- Procurement

**Questions**:
- Where do we create value?
- Where do costs accumulate?
- What can we outsource?
- Where should we integrate?

**Example**: 
- Apple: Design (high value) + manufacturing (outsource)
- Zara: Fast design → production → distribution
- Amazon: Logistics excellence

**Pitfalls**:
- Too detailed = analysis paralysis
- Static view
- Hard to quantify value
- Industry differences

## Core Competencies

**Principle**: Unique bundle of skills and technologies that create customer value and competitive advantage.

**When to Use**:
- Strategic planning
- Resource allocation
- M&A decisions
- Diversification strategy

**Criteria** (Prahalad & Hamel):
- Provides access to wide variety of markets
- Contributes significantly to customer value
- Difficult for competitors to imitate

**Examples**:
- Honda: Engine technology (cars, motorcycles, generators, lawn mowers)
- 3M: Adhesive technology (Post-its, tapes, medical)
- Disney: Family entertainment (movies, parks, merchandise)

**Questions**:
- What are we truly excellent at?
- What's hard to replicate?
- What enables multiple products?
- Are we investing in core competencies?

**Pitfalls**:
- Core rigidities (competencies become constraints)
- Overextension
- Neglecting emerging competencies
- Confusing assets with competencies

## Barriers to Entry

**Principle**: Obstacles that make it difficult for new competitors to enter market.

**When to Use**:
- Industry attractiveness analysis
- Threat assessment
- Strategy formulation
- Investment decisions

**Types**:
- **Economies of Scale**: Size advantages
- **Capital Requirements**: High upfront investment
- **Access to Distribution**: Controlled channels
- **Regulatory**: Licenses, patents, compliance
- **Brand Identity**: Customer loyalty
- **Switching Costs**: Lock-in effects
- **Network Effects**: Value from users

**Questions**:
- How hard is it to enter our market?
- Are barriers rising or falling?
- Can we raise barriers?
- What about digital disruption?

**Example**:
- **High**: Airlines (capital), pharmaceuticals (regulation), telecom (spectrum)
- **Low**: Websites, consulting, retail (pre-Amazon)

**Pitfalls**:
- Barriers can fall suddenly (tech)
- May invite regulation
- Can trap incumbents too
- Complacency

## Competitive Advantage Sources

**Principle**: Systematic identification of why company outperforms rivals.

**When to Use**:
- Strategy formulation
- Competitive analysis
- Sustainability assessment
- Investment thesis

**Sources**:
- **Superior Resources**: Patents, brand, location, talent
- **Superior Capabilities**: Processes, culture, knowledge
- **Superior Position**: Market position, customer relationships
- **Better Execution**: Operations, efficiency

**Sustainability Factors**:
- Hard to imitate
- Non-substitutable
- Rare/valuable
- Organizationally embedded

**Questions**:
- Why do customers choose us?
- Why can't competitors copy?
- How sustainable is advantage?
- Is it eroding?

**Example**:
- Walmart: Distribution system + scale
- Toyota: Production system
- Southwest: Culture + operational excellence

**Pitfalls**:
- Advantages erode
- Success breeds imitation
- Disruption changes game
- Internal politics

## Strategic Groups

**Principle**: Cluster of firms within industry following similar strategies along key dimensions.

**When to Use**:
- Competitive analysis
- Positioning decisions
- Mobility barriers assessment
- Industry mapping

**Dimensions**:
- Price/quality level
- Geographic scope
- Degree of vertical integration
- Product line breadth
- Distribution channels
- Target customer segments

**Analysis**:
- Map competitors on 2-3 dimensions
- Identify clusters
- Assess mobility barriers between groups
- Find strategic space

**Questions**:
- Which strategic group are we in?
- Can we move to better group?
- What are barriers between groups?
- Where are attractive spaces?

**Example**:
- **Airlines**: Major carriers, low-cost, regional, premium
- **Hotels**: Budget, mid-scale, upscale, luxury
- **Retail**: Discount, department, specialty, online

**Pitfalls**:
- Groups can be artificial
- Boundaries blur
- Ignores within-group variation
- Static analysis
