# Law & Policy Models

Legal principles and policy frameworks for governance, regulation, and justice.

## Rule of Law

**Principle**: Law applies equally to all, including those who govern; no one is above the law; legal processes are transparent and predictable.

**When to Use**:
- Governance design
- Institutional building
- Fairness assessment
- Process design

**Questions**:
- Are rules applied consistently?
- Is anyone above the rules?
- Are processes transparent and predictable?

**Example**: Judicial independence, due process, equal protection; corporate governance requiring CEO compliance.

**Pitfalls**:
- Rules can be unjust
- Can become rigid formalism
- Implementation varies widely

---

## Precedent (Stare Decisis)

**Principle**: Prior decisions guide current cases to ensure consistency, predictability, and stability; depart from precedent only with strong justification.

**When to Use**:
- Decision consistency
- Policy making
- Standard setting
- Building on past cases

**Questions**:
- What's been decided before?
- Why should this case be different?
- What precedent am I setting?

**Example**: Legal case law, corporate policy decisions, diplomatic norms; "we've always done it this way" (for good reasons).

**Pitfalls**:
- Can perpetuate bad decisions
- May prevent necessary change
- Context may have shifted

---

## Burden of Proof

**Principle**: Responsibility for proving claims rests on the party making them; standard varies by context (beyond reasonable doubt, preponderance of evidence, etc.).

**When to Use**:
- Argument evaluation
- Evidence requirements
- Decision standards
- Shifting responsibility

**Questions**:
- Who must prove what?
- What standard of proof applies?
- Has the burden been met?

**Example**: Criminal (prosecution must prove guilt); Civil (plaintiff proves harm); Science (hypothesis must be tested).

**Pitfalls**:
- Burden placement can determine outcomes
- Standards can be unclear
- May favor status quo unfairly

---

## Liability

**Principle**: Legal responsibility for harms caused; can be strict (regardless of fault), negligence-based (failure to exercise care), or intentional.

**When to Use**:
- Risk assessment
- Responsibility assignment
- Incentive design
- Contract structuring

**Questions**:
- Who bears the risk of harm?
- What duty of care exists?
- How do liability rules affect behavior?

**Example**: Product liability, professional malpractice, corporate responsibility; "you break it, you buy it."

**Pitfalls**:
- Can lead to defensive behavior
- May be unclear ex ante
- Moral hazard if liability is capped/insured

---

## Due Process

**Principle**: Fair procedures before deprivation of rights; notice, opportunity to be heard, impartial decision-maker.

**When to Use**:
- Disciplinary procedures
- Termination processes
- Fair hearings
- Grievance systems

**Questions**:
- Was there fair notice?
- Was there opportunity to respond?
- Was the decision-maker impartial?

**Example**: Legal trials, employee termination procedures, academic discipline, administrative appeals.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can be slow and costly
- Procedural compliance ≠ substantive justice
- May be gamed

---

## Regulatory Capture

**Principle**: Regulatory agencies may come to serve the interests of the industries they regulate rather than the public interest.

**When to Use**:
- Policy analysis
- Institutional design
- Conflict of interest detection
- Reform advocacy

**Questions**:
- Who influences the regulator?
- Are incentives aligned with public interest?
- Is there revolving door between industry and regulator?

**Example**: FDA drug approval, financial regulation, environmental agencies; also "cognitive capture" (shared worldview).

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to prove
- Some industry expertise is necessary
- Can become cynical conspiracy theory

---

## Nudge / Choice Architecture

**Principle**: Design choices to guide behavior while preserving freedom; defaults, framing, and salience shape decisions without mandates.

**When to Use**:
- Policy design
- User experience
- Behavior change
- Paternalism with liberty

**Questions**:
- What's the default?
- How is information framed?
- What's made easy vs hard?

**Example**: Opt-out organ donation (vs opt-in); auto-enrollment in 401k; calorie labels; default printer settings.

**Pitfalls**:
- Manipulation concerns
- Can backfire if detected
- Who designs the nudge?

---

## Social Contract

**Principle**: Implicit agreement among members of society to cooperate for mutual benefit, surrendering some freedoms for collective security and rights.

**When to Use**:
- Political legitimacy
- Obligation justification
- Community norms
- Institutional design

**Questions**:
- What did we implicitly agree to?
- What legitimizes authority?
- What obligations do we owe each other?

**Example**: Taxes for public goods, obeying laws you didn't personally vote for, mutual defense obligations.

**Pitfalls**:
- Actual consent is fictional
- Who is included in "we"?
- Can justify oppression

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Game Theory → Legal strategy and incentives
- Public Goods → Policy rationale
- Principal-Agent → Regulatory structures
- Ethics → Normative foundations

**Conflicts with**:
- Pure efficiency (justice matters too)
- Informal norms (law vs custom)
- Individual autonomy (collective rules)
