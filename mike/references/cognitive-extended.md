# Cognitive Psychology Models (Extended)

Additional cognitive biases and thinking patterns beyond the core set.

## Recency Bias

**Principle**: Overweighting recent events and underweighting historical patterns.

**When to Use**:
- Investment decisions
- Performance evaluation
- Trend analysis

**Questions**:
- Am I extrapolating from recent data?
- What does long-term history show?
- Is this a trend or noise?

**Example**: Assuming hot stock will keep rising; judging employee on last week vs. full year.

**Mitigation**:
- Review longer time periods
- Weight historical data appropriately
- Distinguish signal from noise

---

## Hindsight Bias

**Principle**: "I knew it all along" - believing past events were more predictable than they were.

**When to Use**:
- Learning from mistakes
- Evaluating decisions
- Understanding history

**Questions**:
- Did I really predict this?
- What did I actually know beforehand?
- Am I rewriting history?

**Example**: After market crash, claiming "I saw it coming"; post-game analysis of "obvious" plays.

**Mitigation**:
- Document predictions in advance
- Review actual vs. claimed foresight
- Recognize uncertainty ex-ante

---

## Overconfidence Bias

**Principle**: Systematically overestimating our knowledge, abilities, and predictions.

**When to Use**:
- Planning and forecasting
- Skill assessment
- Risk evaluation

**Questions**:
- How confident am I vs. historical accuracy?
- What's my confidence interval?
- Am I accounting for unknown unknowns?

**Example**: 90% of drivers rate themselves above average; overly narrow prediction ranges.

**Mitigation**:
- Track calibration over time
- Widen confidence intervals
- Seek external validation

---

## Fundamental Attribution Error

**Principle**: Overattributing others' behavior to character, underweighting situational factors.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding behavior
- Conflict resolution
- Performance evaluation

**Questions**:
- What situational factors might explain this?
- Would I behave differently in same situation?
- Am I judging character vs. circumstances?

**Example**: "They're lazy" vs. "They're dealing with family crisis"; road rage from dispositional attribution.

**Mitigation**:
- Consider situational context
- Apply same lens to self and others
- Look for environmental explanations

---

## Status Quo Bias

**Principle**: Preference for current state; resistance to change even when beneficial.

**When to Use**:
- Change management
- Decision-making
- Understanding resistance

**Questions**:
- Am I defending status quo rationally?
- What would I choose if starting fresh?
- Is stability worth the opportunity cost?

**Example**: Keeping default settings; not switching better providers; organizational inertia.

**Mitigation**:
- Use zero-based thinking
- Quantify switching costs vs. benefits
- Overcome loss aversion

---

## Bandwagon Effect

**Principle**: Tendency to believe/do things because many others do.

**When to Use**:
- Independent thinking
- Investment decisions
- Understanding social proof

**Questions**:
- Am I following the crowd?
- What's my independent analysis?
- Is popularity evidence of correctness?

**Example**: Investment bubbles; fashion trends; "everyone's doing it" justifications.

**Mitigation**:
- Form independent judgment first
- Distinguish wisdom of crowds from herding
- Question popular narratives

---

## Halo Effect

**Principle**: One positive trait influences perception of other unrelated traits.

**When to Use**:
- Hiring decisions
- Product evaluation
- Understanding biases

**Questions**:
- Am I generalizing from one attribute?
- What evidence supports other qualities?
- Is attractiveness influencing judgment?

**Example**: Assuming attractive person is intelligent; successful CEO must be good at everything.

**Mitigation**:
- Evaluate traits independently
- Use structured assessment
- Seek contradictory evidence

---

## Self-Serving Bias

**Principle**: Attributing success to self, failure to external factors.

**When to Use**:
- Learning from outcomes
- Performance review
- Understanding feedback

**Questions**:
- Am I taking credit unfairly?
- What external factors aided success?
- What did I contribute to failure?

**Example**: Good grade = my smarts; bad grade = teacher's fault; team success vs. individual failure.

**Mitigation**:
- Balanced attribution
- Acknowledge luck and help
- Own mistakes

---

## Planning Fallacy

**Principle**: Underestimating time, costs, risks of future actions despite past experience.

**When to Use**:
- Project planning
- Time management
- Resource allocation

**Questions**:
- How long did similar tasks take?
- Am I accounting for complications?
- What's my historical accuracy?

**Example**: Home renovations take 3x longer; software projects always delayed.

**Mitigation**:
- Reference class forecasting
- Add buffers systematically
- Learn from past estimates

---

## Narrative Fallacy

**Principle**: Creating stories to explain events, even when randomness or complexity is real cause.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding explanations
- Evaluating theories
- Critical thinking

**Questions**:
- Is this story too neat?
- Could randomness explain this?
- Am I forcing causality?

**Example**: Market moves explained post-hoc; success stories ignoring survivorship bias.

**Mitigation**:
- Acknowledge randomness
- Test causal claims
- Resist compelling narratives

---

## Curse of Knowledge

**Principle**: Once you know something, hard to imagine not knowing it.

**When to Use**:
- Teaching and communication
- Product design
- Documentation

**Questions**:
- What did I not know before learning this?
- Am I assuming too much knowledge?
- How would a beginner see this?

**Example**: Experts can't explain simply; "obvious" interfaces that confuse users.

**Mitigation**:
- Test with beginners
- Document assumptions
- Explain from first principles

---

## Endowment Effect

**Principle**: Valuing things more once we own them.

**When to Use**:
- Selling decisions
- Negotiation
- Understanding valuations

**Questions**:
- Would I buy this at current price?
- Am I overvaluing due to ownership?
- What's the objective market value?

**Example**: Selling house for more than would pay; garage full of "valuable" junk.

**Mitigation**:
- Use market comparisons
- Apply buying test
- Recognize emotional attachment

---

## Survivorship Bias

**Principle**: Focusing on successes that "survived" while ignoring failures that didn't.

**When to Use**:
- Learning from examples
- Strategy analysis
- Understanding outcomes

**Questions**:
- What failures am I not seeing?
- How many tried and failed?
- Is success rate actually higher?

**Example**: Successful entrepreneurs' advice (ignoring failed ones); WWII plane armor analysis.

**Mitigation**:
- Seek failure data
- Calculate base rates
- Study full population

---

## Illusion of Control

**Principle**: Overestimating ability to influence outcomes beyond actual control.

**When to Use**:
- Risk assessment
- Strategic planning
- Understanding limits

**Questions**:
- What can I actually control?
- Am I conflating correlation and causation?
- What's determined by luck?

**Example**: Gamblers' superstitions; micromanagement; excessive planning for uncertainty.

**Mitigation**:
- Distinguish controllable factors
- Accept uncertainty
- Focus energy appropriately

---

## Clustering Illusion

**Principle**: Seeing patterns in random data.

**When to Use**:
- Data analysis
- Pattern recognition
- Statistical thinking

**Questions**:
- Could this be random?
- What's the expected pattern in noise?
- Am I seeing faces in clouds?

**Example**: Sports "hot hands"; investment patterns; conspiracy theories.

**Mitigation**:
- Test statistical significance
- Understand randomness
- Seek replication

---

## Choice Overload

**Principle**: Too many options leads to worse decisions or decision paralysis.

**When to Use**:
- Product design
- Decision-making
- Understanding behavior

**Questions**:
- Are too many options harmful?
- What's the optimal number?
- Can I simplify choices?

**Example**: Jam study (24 vs. 6 options); retirement plan paralysis.

**Mitigation**:
- Limit options strategically
- Use defaults wisely
- Provide decision aids

---

## Mere Exposure Effect

**Principle**: Preference increases with familiarity.

**When to Use**:
- Marketing
- Understanding preferences
- Building relationships

**Questions**:
- Am I preferring familiar over better?
- Is this actually good or just familiar?
- How does exposure affect judgment?

**Example**: Musical tastes; brand loyalty; political incumbency advantage.

**Mitigation**:
- Try unfamiliar options
- Evaluate objectively
- Distinguish familiarity from quality
