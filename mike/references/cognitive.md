# Cognitive Psychology Models

Understanding cognitive biases and how our minds work.

## Confirmation Bias

**Principle**: Tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information that confirms pre-existing beliefs.

**When to Use**:
- Validating your own analysis
- Peer review and critique
- Research and investigation

**Questions**:
- What evidence would prove me wrong?
- Am I seeking confirming or disconfirming evidence?
- What do critics of this view say?

**Example**: Only reading news that aligns with political views; ignoring data that contradicts investment thesis.

**Mitigation**:
- Actively seek disconfirming evidence
- Use "steel man" (strongest version) of opposing arguments
- Pre-commit to decision criteria before gathering evidence

## Availability Heuristic

**Principle**: Overestimating likelihood of events that are easily recalled or vivid.

**When to Use**:
- Risk assessment
- Probability estimation
- Media analysis

**Questions**:
- Is this actually common, or just memorable?
- Am I influenced by recent/dramatic examples?
- What does the base rate data say?

**Example**: Overestimating plane crash risk after news coverage; underestimating common but undramatic risks.

**Mitigation**:
- Look up actual statistics
- Consider boring but common events
- Be aware of media coverage bias

---

## Anchoring Effect

**Principle**: Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered.

**When to Use**:
- Negotiation
- Estimation and forecasting
- Price setting

**Questions**:
- What was the first number I saw?
- How is that influencing my thinking?
- What if I started with a different reference point?

**Example**: First salary offer in negotiation sets the range; initial price estimate biases final budget.

**Mitigation**:
- Generate independent estimates before seeing anchors
- Deliberately consider multiple reference points
- Be aware of strategic anchoring by others

---

## Framing Effect

**Principle**: Different presentations of the same information lead to different decisions.

**When to Use**:
- Communication and persuasion
- Decision analysis
- Understanding reactions

**Questions**:
- How is this being framed?
- What would the opposite framing look like?
- What's the objective reality beneath the framing?

**Example**:
- "90% survival rate" vs "10% mortality rate"
- "Save $50" vs "Avoid losing $50"
- "Gain-framed" vs "loss-framed" messaging

**Mitigation**:
- Reframe the same situation multiple ways
- Focus on objective data
- Be aware of emotional framing

## Dunning-Kruger Effect

**Principle**: People with low competence overestimate their ability; experts underestimate theirs.

**When to Use**:
- Self-assessment
- Evaluating confidence levels
- Learning and skill development

**Questions**:
- How much do I actually know about this?
- Am I on "Mount Stupid" or in the "Valley of Despair"?
- What would an expert say about my assessment?

**Stages**:
1. **Peak of "Mount Stupid"**: Beginner overconfidence
2. **Valley of Despair**: Awareness of how much you don't know
3. **Slope of Enlightenment**: Growing competence and confidence
4. **Plateau of Sustainability**: Expert realistic confidence

**Mitigation**:
- Seek expert feedback
- Test your knowledge rigorously
- Embrace "I don't know"

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Principle**: Continuing an endeavor because of previously invested resources, despite current costs exceeding benefits.

**When to Use**:
- Project continuation decisions
- Relationship and career choices
- Investment decisions

**Questions**:
- If I started fresh today, would I make this choice?
- Am I holding on because of past investment?
- What matters: past costs or future value?

**Example**: Continuing a failing project because "we've already spent $1M"; staying in a bad relationship because "we've been together 5 years".

**Mitigation**:
- Evaluate decisions based on future costs/benefits only
- Use "zero-based" thinking: assume you're starting fresh
- Recognize emotional attachment to past investment

---

## Mental Accounting

**Principle**: Treating money differently based on arbitrary categories or sources.

**When to Use**:
- Personal finance
- Understanding consumer behavior
- Resource allocation

**Questions**:
- Would I make this decision if the money source was different?
- Am I treating fungible resources as non-fungible?
- What's the economically rational choice?

**Example**: Spending windfall gains more freely than earned income; unwillingness to "break" large bills; treating investment gains differently than salary.

**Mitigation**:
- Remember money is fungible
- Evaluate decisions on absolute terms
- Use consistent decision criteria regardless of source

---

## Loss Aversion

**Principle**: Losses hurt approximately twice as much as equivalent gains feel good.

**When to Use**:
- Risk assessment
- Pricing and framing
- Understanding resistance to change

**Questions**:
- Am I being overly cautious due to loss aversion?
- What's the symmetric gain/loss evaluation?
- Is the fear of loss preventing rational risk-taking?

**Example**: Holding losing stocks too long to avoid realizing loss; requiring 2:1 gain:loss ratio to take fair bets.

**Mitigation**:
- Evaluate risks symmetrically
- Pre-commit to decision rules
- Consider opportunity costs of excessive caution
