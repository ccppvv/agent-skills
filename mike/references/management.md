# Organization & Management Models

## Span of Control
**Principle**: Number of direct reports a manager can effectively manage (typically 5-9).
**Application**: Organization design; management structure; delegation.
**Example**: Flat vs. hierarchical orgs; manager capacity limits.

## Conway's Law
**Principle**: System design mirrors organization's communication structure.
**Application**: System architecture; team organization; product design.
**Example**: Microservices match team structure; monoliths from centralized teams.

## Parkinson's Law
**Principle**: Work expands to fill time available.
**Application**: Project planning; time management; deadline setting.
**Example**: Tasks taking full allocated time; artificial deadlines improving efficiency.

## Brooks's Law
**Principle**: Adding people to late project makes it later.
**Application**: Project management; team scaling; crisis response.
**Example**: Onboarding overhead; communication complexity; mythical man-month.

## Dunbar's Number
**Principle**: ~150 stable social relationships limit.
**Application**: Organization size; team structure; community design.
**Example**: Gore-Tex 150-person facilities; tribe sizes; relationship capacity.

## Two-Pizza Team
**Principle**: Teams small enough to feed with two pizzas (~6-10 people).
**Application**: Team sizing; agile methodology; autonomy.
**Example**: Amazon teams; startup squads; autonomous units.

## Maker vs. Manager Schedule
**Principle**: Different work modes require different time organization.
**Application**: Calendar management; productivity; meeting culture.
**Example**: Developers need blocks; managers handle interruptions; context switching costs.

## Radical Candor
**Principle**: Care personally while challenging directly.
**Application**: Feedback; management; relationships.
**Example**: Honest feedback with empathy; caring criticism; direct communication.

## Psychological Safety
**Principle**: Team environment where interpersonal risk-taking is safe.
**Application**: Team performance; innovation; learning culture.
**Example**: Google Project Aristotle; speak-up culture; mistake tolerance.

## Theory X vs. Theory Y
**Principle**: Assumptions about employee motivation shape management style.
**Application**: Leadership philosophy; org culture; management approach.
**Example**: Micromanagement vs. autonomy; control vs. trust.

## Servant Leadership
**Principle**: Leader serves team rather than commanding.
**Application**: Leadership style; team empowerment; culture building.
**Example**: Removing obstacles; enabling success; bottom-up leadership.

## OKRs (Objectives & Key Results)
**Principle**: Goal-setting framework with objectives and measurable results.
**Application**: Strategy execution; alignment; measurement.
**Example**: Google's system; quarterly goals; outcome focus.

## RACI Matrix
**Principle**: Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed role clarity.
**Application**: Decision-making; project management; accountability.
**Example**: Cross-functional projects; responsibility mapping; decision rights.

## Eisenhower Matrix
**Principle**: Prioritize by urgency and importance.
**Application**: Time management; prioritization; delegation.
**Example**: Important-not-urgent gets scheduled; urgent-not-important gets delegated.

## Peter Principle
**Principle**: People promoted to level of incompetence.
**Application**: Promotion decisions; career planning; organization dysfunction.
**Example**: Great engineer promoted to bad manager; competence-based promotion limits.

## Principal-Agent Problem

**Principle**: When one party (agent) makes decisions on behalf of another (principal), conflicts arise due to misaligned incentives.

**When to Use**:
- Organizational design
- Compensation structure
- Corporate governance
- Monitoring and control

**Examples**:
- Shareholders (principals) vs CEOs (agents)
- Patients (principals) vs doctors (agents)
- Clients (principals) vs lawyers (agents)

**Misalignment Sources**:
- Different risk preferences
- Information asymmetry
- Effort not observable
- Different time horizons

**Solutions**:
- Alignment incentives (stock options)
- Monitoring and reporting
- Performance metrics
- Reputation effects

**Pitfalls**:
- Over-monitoring reduces autonomy
- Wrong incentives cause gaming
- Can't eliminate all conflicts

## Conway's Law

**Principle**: Organizations design systems that mirror their communication structure.

**When to Use**:
- Software architecture
- Organizational design
- Team structure
- System boundaries

**Statement**: "Any organization that designs a system will produce a design whose structure is a copy of the organization's communication structure."

**Implications**:
- Team boundaries become system boundaries
- Communication paths become dependencies
- Org structure constrains design
- Inverse Conway: Design org for desired architecture

**Example**: 
- 4 teams → 4-component system
- Siloed departments → siloed systems
- Microservices require autonomous teams

**Pitfalls**:
- May perpetuate poor structures
- Hard to change org structure
- Doesn't account for good design

## Parkinson's Law

**Principle**: Work expands to fill the time available for its completion.

**When to Use**:
- Time management
- Deadline setting
- Project planning
- Productivity improvement

**Corollaries**:
- Data expands to fill storage available
- Bureaucracy grows regardless of need
- Expenses rise to meet income

**Questions**:
- Is this task really necessary?
- What's the minimum time needed?
- Am I padding estimates?
- Can we set tighter deadlines?

**Example**: Task given 2 weeks takes 2 weeks; same task given 2 days might take 2 days. Meetings fill allotted time.

**Solutions**:
- Set tight (but reasonable) deadlines
- Timeboxing
- Regular pruning
- Focus on essentials

**Pitfalls**:
- Can lead to rushed work
- Quality may suffer
- Burnout risk

## Dunbar's Number

**Principle**: Cognitive limit of ~150 stable social relationships one can maintain.

**When to Use**:
- Team sizing
- Organizational structure
- Community management
- Social network design

**Layers**:
- 5: Close friends
- 15: Good friends
- 50: Friends
- 150: Meaningful contacts
- 500: Acquaintances

**Implications**:
- Teams over 150 need hierarchy
- Direct relationships don't scale
- Communication overhead grows
- Informal coordination breaks down

**Example**: Military units (~150); Gore-Tex facilities (<150 people); Spotify squads/tribes.

**Pitfalls**:
- Number varies by person
- Technology may change limits
- Quality vs quantity tradeoff

## Delegation

**Principle**: Assign tasks and decision authority to others while retaining accountability.

**When to Use**:
- Scaling leadership
- Developing people
- Time management
- Empowerment

**Levels**:
1. Do exactly as I say
2. Research and recommend
3. Decide and inform me
4. Act independently, report periodically
5. Full authority

**Questions**:
- Can someone else do this 70% as well?
- Is this best use of my time?
- Who would benefit from learning this?
- What authority do they need?

**Example**: Manager delegates routine approvals but retains strategic decisions. Clear expectations + authority + support.

**Pitfalls**:
- Delegating without authority
- Micromanaging after delegation
- No follow-up
- Abdicating vs delegating

## Feedback Culture

**Principle**: Create environment where continuous, constructive feedback flows freely.

**When to Use**:
- Performance management
- Team development
- Continuous improvement
- Psychological safety building

**Elements**:
- **Timely**: Close to event
- **Specific**: Concrete behaviors
- **Actionable**: Clear next steps
- **Balanced**: Positive and constructive
- **Two-way**: Manager ↔ Team

**Types**:
- Appreciation: Recognize value
- Coaching: Improve performance
- Evaluation: Rate against standards

**Example**: Radical Candor (care personally + challenge directly); regular 1-on-1s; retrospectives; peer feedback.

**Pitfalls**:
- Feedback without safety
- Only negative feedback
- Too infrequent
- No action on feedback

## Agile Methodology

**Principle**: Iterative development with frequent feedback, adaptation, and collaboration.

**When to Use**:
- Uncertain requirements
- Fast-changing environments
- Complex products
- User-centered development

**Values** (Agile Manifesto):
- Individuals > processes
- Working software > documentation
- Customer collaboration > contracts
- Responding to change > following plan

**Practices**:
- Sprints (1-4 weeks)
- Daily standups
- Sprint planning/review/retro
- Product backlog
- Cross-functional teams

**Example**: Scrum, Kanban, XP; software teams shipping every sprint; rapid pivots based on feedback.

**Pitfalls**:
- "Agile" without agility
- No discipline
- Cargo cult practices
- Ignoring technical quality

## Span of Attention

**Principle**: Limited cognitive capacity to focus on and process information simultaneously.

**When to Use**:
- Meeting design
- Communication planning
- Presentation structure
- Information architecture

**Limits**:
- ~7±2 items in working memory
- ~20 minutes focus before break needed
- 1-3 priorities max
- Single-tasking > multi-tasking

**Questions**:
- Am I overwhelming with information?
- Can this be chunked?
- What's the one key message?
- Are we trying to do too much?

**Example**: Meeting agendas with 3 topics max; presentations in chunks; "Three Priorities" rule; focused sprints.

**Pitfalls**:
- Information overload
- Too many priorities = no priorities
- Assuming others can track everything

## Brooks's Law

**Principle**: Adding people to a late software project makes it later.

**When to Use**:
- Project recovery attempts
- Resource planning
- Scaling teams
- Timeline management

**Reasons**:
- Ramp-up time
- Communication overhead (n² connections)
- Knowledge transfer cost
- Integration complexity

**Formula**: Person-months are not fungible; 9 women can't have a baby in 1 month.

**Questions**:
- Will adding people help or hurt?
- What's the communication cost?
- Is the task parallelizable?
- Do we have time for onboarding?

**Example**: Late project adds 5 developers → existing team spends time training → project delays more.

**Pitfalls**:
- Assumes late = understaffed
- Sometimes adding people does help
- Applies more to complex, interdependent work

## Theory X vs Theory Y

**Principle**: Managers' assumptions about employees affect management style and outcomes.

**When to Use**:
- Leadership development
- Culture design
- Motivation strategy
- Organizational philosophy

**Theory X** (Traditional):
- Assumptions: People dislike work, avoid responsibility, need control
- Management: Strict supervision, punishment/rewards, micromanagement
- Results: Self-fulfilling prophecy

**Theory Y** (Humanistic):
- Assumptions: Work is natural, people seek responsibility, creativity is common
- Management: Trust, autonomy, purpose, mastery
- Results: Engagement and initiative

**Questions**:
- What do I assume about my team?
- Am I managing to worst case or best case?
- What behavior am I reinforcing?

**Example**: Theory X → time tracking, approvals. Theory Y → results focus, autonomy, trust.

**Pitfalls**:
- Pure Theory Y can be naive
- Context matters
- Need accountability either way

## Maker vs Manager Schedule

**Principle**: Different work requires different time structures - long blocks vs. meetings.

**When to Use**:
- Calendar management
- Team coordination
- Productivity optimization
- Understanding conflicts

**Maker Schedule**:
- Long uninterrupted blocks (half-day)
- Deep work on complex problems
- Context switching is costly
- Flow states critical

**Manager Schedule**:
- Hour-long slots
- Many short interactions
- Coordination and decisions
- Responsive to needs

**Conflicts**:
- 1-hour meeting disrupts maker's entire day
- Makers seem unresponsive to managers
- Async communication helps

**Solutions**:
- Meeting-free days for makers
- Office hours
- Batched meetings
- Respect maker time

**Example**: Engineers need morning blocks; managers have back-to-back; compromise on afternoon meetings.

**Pitfalls**:
- Everyone thinks they're a maker
- Some maker work benefits from breaks
- Extreme isolation hurts collaboration
