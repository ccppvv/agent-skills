# Game Theory & Negotiation Models

Models for strategic interaction, cooperation, and conflict resolution.

## Nash Equilibrium

**Principle**: A stable state where no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing strategy.

**When to Use**:
- Strategic analysis
- Predicting behavior
- Market dynamics

**Questions**:
- What's each player's best response to others' strategies?
- Is there a stable equilibrium?
- How can I move to a better equilibrium?

**Example**: Traffic congestion (everyone taking shortest route), pricing competition, arms races.

**Pitfalls**:
- May be multiple equilibria
- Equilibrium ≠ optimal outcome
- Assumes perfect rationality

---

## Prisoner's Dilemma

**Principle**: Individual rationality leads to collective suboptimality when cooperation isn't enforceable.

**When to Use**:
- Cooperation challenges
- Trust problems
- Policy design

**Questions**:
- What prevents cooperation here?
- How can we align individual and collective interests?
- What mechanisms enforce cooperation?

**Example**: Climate change, overfishing, doping in sports, arms races.

**Pitfalls**:
- Repeated games enable cooperation
- Real situations have more complexity
- Communication changes dynamics

---

## BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)

**Principle**: Your negotiation power depends on your best option if negotiations fail.

**When to Use**:
- Negotiation preparation
- Power assessment
- Walk-away decisions

**Questions**:
- What's my BATNA?
- What's their BATNA?
- How can I improve my BATNA before negotiating?

**Example**: Job offers (competing offers strengthen position), supplier negotiations, real estate.

**Pitfalls**:
- BATNA may be unclear
- Can be bluffed about
- Ignores relationship value

---

## ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

**Principle**: The range where both parties can reach agreement - overlap between reservation prices.

**When to Use**:
- Deal feasibility assessment
- Negotiation strategy
- Value creation

**Questions**:
- Where do our acceptable ranges overlap?
- Is there a ZOPA at all?
- How can we expand the ZOPA?

**Example**: Salary negotiations (your minimum vs their maximum), sales deals, partnerships.

**Pitfalls**:
- Information asymmetry hides ZOPA
- Can be manipulated by anchoring
- Assumes win-lose frame

---

## Win-Win Thinking

**Principle**: Focus on creating value and expanding the pie rather than just dividing it.

**When to Use**:
- Integrative bargaining
- Long-term relationships
- Creative problem-solving

**Questions**:
- What do they value that costs me little?
- What do I value that costs them little?
- How can we both be better off?

**Example**: Mergers (1+1=3), cross-licensing, resource swaps.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all situations are win-win
- Can be exploited by zero-sum thinkers
- May miss distributive issues

---

## Commitment Devices

**Principle**: Intentionally constraining future options to make threats/promises credible.

**When to Use**:
- Credibility building
- Self-control
- Strategic positioning

**Questions**:
- How can I make my position credible?
- What makes this commitment costly to break?
- Is this constraint worth it?

**Example**: Burning bridges, public pledges, contractual penalties, Ulysses pacts.

**Pitfalls**:
- Limits flexibility
- May backfire if circumstances change
- Can escalate conflicts

---

## Cheap Talk

**Principle**: Costless claims lack credibility without supporting evidence or incentives for honesty.

**When to Use**:
- Evaluating claims
- Signaling strategy
- Communication design

**Questions**:
- Does this claim cost anything to make?
- What incentives support honesty?
- How can I make talk costly (and credible)?

**Example**: Political promises, unverifiable reviews, talk without action.

**Pitfalls**:
- Some cheap talk works (communication norms)
- Costly signaling isn't always better
- Can be too cynical

---

## Signaling

**Principle**: Costly, hard-to-fake actions reveal private information credibly.

**When to Use**:
- Information revelation
- Reputation building
- Screening mechanisms

**Questions**:
- What's the cost of this signal?
- Can it be faked?
- What information does it reveal?

**Example**: Education (costly signal of ability), warranties (signal of quality), peacock's tail.

**Pitfalls**:
- Signals can be wasteful
- Arms races in signaling
- May not reveal desired trait

---

## Ultimatum Game

**Principle**: People reject unfair offers even at personal cost, revealing fairness preferences.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding fairness norms
- Offer design
- Cultural differences

**Questions**:
- What's perceived as fair here?
- Will they accept an economically rational but "unfair" offer?
- How much do fairness concerns matter?

**Example**: Salary offers, profit sharing, tipping, price fairness.

**Pitfalls**:
- Fairness norms vary by context
- Repeated games differ
- Culture significantly affects rejection rates

---

## Zero-Sum vs Non-Zero-Sum

**Principle**: Zero-sum: one's gain is another's loss. Non-zero-sum: mutual gains (or losses) possible.

**When to Use**:
- Framing situations correctly
- Identifying value creation opportunities
- Cooperation assessment

**Questions**:
- Is this truly zero-sum or do I just perceive it that way?
- Where can we create value together?
- What makes this competitive vs cooperative?

**Example**: Zero-sum: poker, fixed pie division. Non-zero-sum: trade, innovation, relationships.

**Pitfalls**:
- Easy to mistake non-zero for zero-sum
- Some hybrid situations exist
- Framing affects behavior

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Incentives → Understanding strategic behavior
- Psychology → Real human behavior vs game theory assumptions
- Systems Thinking → Multi-player, long-term dynamics
- Information Asymmetry → Strategic information revelation

**Conflicts with**:
- Pure altruism assumptions
- Perfect information models
- Irrationality
