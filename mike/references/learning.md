# Learning & Cognitive Science Models

Models for effective learning, skill acquisition, and knowledge transfer.

## Spaced Repetition

**Principle**: Information is best retained when review intervals increase over time, following the forgetting curve.

**When to Use**:
- Long-term retention
- Language learning
- Exam preparation

**Questions**:
- What needs to be remembered long-term?
- When should I review this again?
- Am I reviewing too frequently or infrequently?

**Example**: Anki flashcards, language apps, medical school memorization.

**Pitfalls**:
- Optimizes retention, not understanding
- Requires consistent discipline
- Can become mechanical without comprehension

---

## Deliberate Practice

**Principle**: Improvement requires focused, structured practice on specific weaknesses with immediate feedback.

**When to Use**:
- Skill development
- Performance improvement
- Expertise building

**Questions**:
- What specific weakness am I targeting?
- Am I getting immediate, accurate feedback?
- Is this comfortably challenging (not too easy/hard)?

**Example**: Musicians practicing difficult passages, athletes drilling fundamentals, programmers solving targeted problems.

**Pitfalls**:
- Uncomfortable and effortful
- Requires expert feedback or self-awareness
- Can lead to burnout if overdone

---

## Chunking

**Principle**: Grouping information into meaningful units expands working memory capacity.

**When to Use**:
- Memory enhancement
- Pattern recognition
- Skill automation

**Questions**:
- What larger patterns exist in this information?
- How can I group related items?
- What's the meaningful structure?

**Example**: Phone numbers (chunks of 3-4 digits), chess positions (patterns not individual pieces), programming design patterns.

**Pitfalls**:
- Initial chunking is cognitively demanding
- May miss details within chunks
- Chunks are domain-specific (don't transfer)

---

## Interleaving

**Principle**: Mixing practice of different but related skills enhances learning more than blocked practice.

**When to Use**:
- Skill integration
- Problem-solving training
- Exam preparation

**Questions**:
- Am I practicing skills in isolation or combination?
- What's the natural context where these skills mix?
- How can I vary practice conditions?

**Example**: Math students practicing mixed problem types vs one type at a time; language learners mixing grammar, vocabulary, conversation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Feels less productive initially
- Requires more mental effort
- May confuse complete beginners

---

## Retrieval Practice

**Principle**: Actively recalling information strengthens memory more than passive review.

**When to Use**:
- Study strategies
- Knowledge retention
- Skill consolidation

**Questions**:
- Can I generate this from memory?
- What would I struggle to recall?
- When did I last retrieve this actively?

**Example**: Practice tests, flashcards, teaching others, self-quizzing.

**Pitfalls**:
- Harder and less pleasant than re-reading
- Requires knowing what to retrieve
- Can reinforce errors if feedback lacking

---

## Metacognition

**Principle**: Awareness and understanding of one's own thought processes enhances learning and problem-solving.

**When to Use**:
- Learning strategy optimization
- Error analysis
- Skill development

**Questions**:
- How do I know that I know this?
- What's my thinking process?
- Where do I typically make mistakes?

**Example**: "I understand this concept but struggle to apply it" vs "I don't understand this at all."

**Pitfalls**:
- Requires honest self-assessment
- Dunning-Kruger effect (incompetence → can't recognize incompetence)
- Can become navel-gazing

---

## Transfer of Learning

**Principle**: Applying knowledge/skills from one context to another - easier for abstract principles than specific procedures.

**When to Use**:
- Skill generalization
- Curriculum design
- Problem-solving

**Questions**:
- What underlying principle transfers?
- How is this context similar/different?
- What's domain-specific vs domain-general?

**Example**: Programming logic transferring across languages, mathematical reasoning to physics, negotiation skills to parenting.

**Pitfalls**:
- Transfer often fails (negative transfer)
- Requires recognizing deep structure
- Context-dependent cues may not transfer

---

## Zone of Proximal Development

**Principle**: Optimal learning occurs just beyond current ability level, with appropriate support.

**When to Use**:
- Task difficulty calibration
- Mentoring/coaching
- Curriculum design

**Questions**:
- What can I do with help but not alone?
- Is this too easy, too hard, or just right?
- What scaffold do I need to reach the next level?

**Example**: Reading slightly above level, programming challenges that stretch but don't overwhelm, sports training progressions.

**Pitfalls**:
- ZPD is dynamic (shifts with progress)
- Requires accurate assessment
- Too much scaffolding creates dependency

---

## Scaffolding

**Principle**: Temporary support structures that enable learning, gradually removed as competence develops.

**When to Use**:
- Teaching complex skills
- Onboarding
- Capability building

**Questions**:
- What support enables progress now?
- When can this scaffold be removed?
- Am I creating dependency or enabling independence?

**Example**: Training wheels on bikes, worked examples in math, code templates for beginners.

**Pitfalls**:
- Scaffold removal timing is critical
- May prevent deep understanding
- Can become permanent crutches

---

## Growth Mindset

**Principle**: Believing abilities can develop through effort leads to better learning outcomes than fixed mindset.

**When to Use**:
- Mindset cultivation
- Resilience building
- Feedback framing

**Questions**:
- Do I see this as revealing limits or opportunities?
- Am I focused on proving or improving?
- How do I interpret failures and setbacks?

**Example**: "I can't do this yet" vs "I can't do this"; praising effort vs praising intelligence.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can become empty platitude
- Effort alone isn't sufficient
- May discount genuine constraints

---

## Desirable Difficulties

**Principle**: Introducing certain challenges during learning improves long-term retention and transfer.

**When to Use**:
- Learning design
- Training optimization
- Study strategies

**Questions**:
- What difficulty will help, not just hinder?
- Am I making this too easy?
- What struggle leads to strength?

**Example**: Generation effect (producing answers vs reading), spacing, interleaving, variation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all difficulties are desirable
- Requires calibration to skill level
- Can demotivate if overdone

---

## Elaborative Interrogation

**Principle**: Asking "why" and "how" questions about material deepens understanding and retention.

**When to Use**:
- Deep comprehension
- Critical reading
- Knowledge integration

**Questions**:
- Why is this true?
- How does this relate to what I know?
- What explains this phenomenon?

**Example**: "Why does this function work this way?" "How does this connect to previous concepts?"

**Pitfalls**:
- Time-intensive
- Requires existing knowledge
- Can create false explanations

---

## Bloom's Taxonomy

**Principle**: Cognitive learning objectives arranged in hierarchy from lower-order (remembering) to higher-order thinking skills (creating).

**When to Use**:
- Curriculum design
- Assessment creation
- Learning objective setting

**Questions**:
- What level of cognitive skill am I targeting?
- Am I assessing at the right complexity level?
- How do I scaffold from lower to higher-order thinking?

**Example**: Six levels - Remember (recall facts) → Understand (explain ideas) → Apply (use in new situations) → Analyze (break into parts) → Evaluate (make judgments) → Create (produce new work).

**Pitfalls**:
- Levels aren't strictly hierarchical in practice
- Higher-order doesn't always mean better
- Cultural biases in what counts as "higher" thinking

---

## Constructivism

**Principle**: Learners actively construct knowledge through experience rather than passively receiving it.

**When to Use**:
- Active learning design
- Problem-based learning
- Discovery learning

**Questions**:
- How can learners discover this themselves?
- What experience will build this understanding?
- Am I telling or enabling discovery?

**Example**: Lab experiments vs lecture, project-based learning, Socratic method, learning by doing.

**Pitfalls**:
- Inefficient for established knowledge
- Can lead to misconceptions without guidance
- Requires more time and resources

---

## Experiential Learning (Kolb's Cycle)

**Principle**: Learning is cyclical process through concrete experience → reflection → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation.

**When to Use**:
- Skill development programs
- Leadership training
- Professional development

**Questions**:
- What experience triggered this learning?
- Have I reflected on what happened?
- What general principle can I extract?
- How will I test this next time?

**Example**: Internships, simulations, action learning projects, after-action reviews.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can get stuck in any phase
- Reflection requires psychological safety
- Abstract conceptualization may be weak

---

## Active Learning

**Principle**: Engaging directly with material through activities improves outcomes over passive listening.

**When to Use**:
- Classroom design
- Training programs
- Knowledge retention

**Questions**:
- What can participants do vs just hear?
- How can I make this interactive?
- What activity reinforces the concept?

**Example**: Think-pair-share, case studies, simulations, hands-on exercises, peer teaching.

**Pitfalls**:
- Activity without purpose is busywork
- Requires more preparation
- Can be uncomfortable for some learners

---

## Flipped Classroom

**Principle**: Invert traditional instruction - content delivery at home, active practice in class.

**When to Use**:
- Maximizing in-person time
- Self-paced content delivery
- Active learning emphasis

**Questions**:
- What can be learned independently?
- What needs expert guidance/collaboration?
- How do I ensure pre-work completion?

**Example**: Video lectures at home, problem-solving in class; readings before discussion; tutorials before lab work.

**Pitfalls**:
- Assumes self-directed learners
- Digital divide issues
- Requires high-quality pre-materials

---

## Cognitive Load Theory

**Principle**: Working memory has limited capacity; instructional design should manage intrinsic, extraneous, and germane cognitive load.

**When to Use**:
- Instructional design
- Complex skill teaching
- User experience design

**Questions**:
- Am I overloading working memory?
- What's essential vs distracting?
- How can I build schemas to reduce load?

**Example**: Worked examples, progressive complexity, eliminating unnecessary information, integrated diagrams.

**Pitfalls**:
- What's "extraneous" depends on expertise
- Oversimplification can prevent deep learning
- Individual differences in capacity

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Systems Thinking → Understanding learning as system
- Feedback Loops → Improvement cycles
- First Principles → Building from fundamentals
- Deliberate Practice + Metacognition → Optimal skill building

**Conflicts with**:
- Quick-fix learning approaches
- Pure memorization
- Passive consumption
