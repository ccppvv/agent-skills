# Marketing & Product Models

## Product-Market Fit
**Principle**: Product satisfies strong market demand.
**Application**: Startup validation; product strategy; market assessment.
**Example**: When customers spontaneously refer; retention metrics high; organic growth.

## Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
**Principle**: Simplest version that validates core hypothesis.
**Application**: Lean startup; rapid validation; resource efficiency.
**Example**: Landing page before product; wizard of oz testing; concierge MVP.

## Innovation Adoption Curve
**Principle**: Innovators → Early Adopters → Early/Late Majority → Laggards.
**Application**: Go-to-market; product positioning; marketing strategy.
**Example**: Tech products start with enthusiasts; mainstream requires different messaging.

## Positioning
**Principle**: Distinct place in customer's mind relative to alternatives.
**Application**: Brand strategy; differentiation; messaging.
**Example**: Volvo=safety; Apple=innovation; Walmart=low-prices.

## Segmentation
**Principle**: Dividing market into distinct customer groups.
**Application**: Targeting; product development; marketing.
**Example**: B2B vs. B2C; demographics; psychographics; use cases.

## Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)
**Principle**: Total revenue from customer relationship.
**Application**: Acquisition economics; retention strategy; pricing.
**Example**: LTV > CAC = sustainable; subscription value; repeat purchases.

## Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
**Principle**: Cost to acquire one customer.
**Application**: Marketing efficiency; channel optimization; unit economics.
**Example**: Ad spend per customer; sales cost; marketing ROI.

## Viral Coefficient (K-Factor)
**Principle**: Average referrals per user; K>1 = viral growth.
**Application**: Growth strategy; product design; network effects.
**Example**: Dropbox referrals; social sharing; growth loops.

## North Star Metric
**Principle**: Single metric best predicting long-term success.
**Application**: Focus; alignment; product strategy.
**Example**: Facebook MAU; Airbnb nights booked; Amazon purchases.

## Pirate Metrics (AARRR)
**Principle**: Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Revenue → Referral.
**Application**: Growth funnel; metric framework; optimization.
**Example**: Startup metrics; growth hacking; funnel analysis.

## Brand Equity
**Principle**: Value premium from brand recognition/reputation.
**Application**: Pricing power; competitive advantage; marketing investment.
**Example**: Apple premium; Nike brand; Coca-Cola value.

## Word of Mouth
**Principle**: Person-to-person recommendation driving adoption.
**Application**: Growth strategy; product excellence; community building.
**Example**: Tesla no ads; viral products; customer evangelists.

## Price Elasticity
**Principle**: Demand responsiveness to price changes.
**Application**: Pricing strategy; revenue optimization; market understanding.
**Example**: Luxury goods less elastic; commodities more elastic.

## Freemium
**Principle**: Free basic version, paid premium features.
**Application**: SaaS strategy; user acquisition; conversion optimization.
**Example**: Spotify, LinkedIn, Dropbox.

## Two-Sided Marketplace
**Principle**: Platform connecting two distinct user groups.
**Application**: Platform strategy; network effects; chicken-egg problem.
**Example**: Uber (riders/drivers); Airbnb (guests/hosts); eBay (buyers/sellers).

## AIDA Model

**Principle**: Customer journey through Attention → Interest → Desire → Action.

**When to Use**:
- Marketing campaigns
- Sales process design
- Content creation
- Conversion optimization

**Stages**:
1. **Attention**: Grab awareness (headlines, ads, visuals)
2. **Interest**: Build engagement (features, benefits, stories)
3. **Desire**: Create want (emotional appeal, social proof, scarcity)
4. **Action**: Trigger behavior (CTA, easy purchase, urgency)

**Questions**:
- How do we grab attention?
- What builds interest?
- What creates desire?
- What prevents action?

**Example**: Ad grabs attention → Landing page builds interest → Testimonials create desire → "Buy now" button triggers action.

**Pitfalls**:
- Linear model (reality is messy)
- Ignores retention
- Different channels need different approaches

## Viral Loops

**Principle**: Product growth through users bringing new users in self-perpetuating cycle.

**When to Use**:
- Growth strategy
- Product design
- User acquisition
- Network effects

**Components**:
- User experience triggers sharing
- Invitation mechanism
- New user onboarding
- Cycle repeats

**Viral Coefficient (K)**:
- K > 1: Exponential growth
- K = 1: Linear growth
- K < 1: Decay

**Example**: 
- Dropbox: Extra storage for referrals
- Zoom: Join meeting invites new users
- PayPal: Send money invites recipient
- Instagram: Share to other platforms

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to engineer
- Can feel spammy
- May attract wrong users
- Network saturation

## Customer Lifetime Value (CLV/LTV)

**Principle**: Total profit expected from customer over entire relationship.

**When to Use**:
- Acquisition cost decisions
- Pricing strategy
- Customer segmentation
- Investment prioritization

**Formula**: 
CLV = (Average Purchase Value × Purchase Frequency × Customer Lifespan) - Acquisition/Service Costs

**Rule**: CLV should be >> CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
- Good ratio: CLV/CAC > 3:1

**Questions**:
- How much can we spend to acquire?
- Which customers are most valuable?
- How to increase CLV?

**Example**: SaaS: $100/month × 24 months = $2,400 LTV. Can spend up to $800 on acquisition.

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to predict lifespan
- Assumes past = future
- Ignores churn variations

## Marketing Funnel

**Principle**: Visualization of customer journey from awareness to purchase.

**When to Use**:
- Campaign planning
- Conversion optimization
- Understanding drop-offs
- Resource allocation

**Stages**:
- **Awareness**: Know brand exists
- **Interest**: Learn more
- **Consideration**: Compare options
- **Intent**: Decide to buy
- **Purchase**: Convert
- **Loyalty**: Repeat/advocate

**Metrics at Each Stage**:
- Top: Reach, impressions
- Middle: Engagement, leads
- Bottom: Conversions, sales

**Questions**:
- Where are people dropping off?
- What moves people to next stage?
- Which stage needs most attention?

**Example**: 10,000 aware → 1,000 interested → 100 considering → 10 purchase. Optimize weakest stage.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not always linear
- Mobile vs desktop differences
- Multi-touch attribution complex

## Tribe Marketing

**Principle**: Build communities around shared identity and values, not just products.

**When to Use**:
- Brand building
- Community management
- Loyalty creation
- Differentiation

**Elements**:
- Shared beliefs/values
- Common identity
- Exclusive belonging
- Leader/evangelist
- Rituals and language

**Questions**:
- Who do customers become by choosing us?
- What do they stand for?
- What's the shared identity?
- How do we facilitate connection?

**Example**: Harley-Davidson riders; Apple fans; Crossfit community; Peloton tribe; "I'm a Mac" identity.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can't be manufactured
- Exclusivity can limit growth
- Tribes can turn toxic
- Authenticity required

## Scarcity Principle

**Principle**: Limited availability increases perceived value and urgency.

**When to Use**:
- Promotions
- Product launches
- Conversion optimization
- Creating urgency

**Types**:
- **Time-limited**: Deadline creates urgency
- **Quantity-limited**: Limited stock
- **Access-limited**: Exclusivity
- **Seasonal**: Only available certain times

**Mechanisms**:
- Loss aversion (FOMO)
- Social proof (others want it)
- Psychological reactance (can't have = want more)

**Example**: "Only 3 left"; "Sale ends tonight"; Limited edition; Exclusive access; Supreme drops.

**Pitfalls**:
- Overuse reduces credibility
- False scarcity is deceptive
- Can backfire if seen as manipulation
- May train customers to wait

## Pirate Metrics (AARRR)

**Principle**: Five key metrics for startup growth: Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue.

**When to Use**:
- Startup metrics
- Growth strategy
- Funnel optimization
- Investor communication

**Framework**:
- **Acquisition**: Users find you (traffic, signups)
- **Activation**: First good experience (aha moment)
- **Retention**: Users come back (engagement, churn)
- **Referral**: Users tell others (viral growth)
- **Revenue**: Monetization (ARPU, LTV)

**Questions**:
- Which stage is the bottleneck?
- Where to focus optimization?
- What's the biggest lever?

**Example**: SaaS dashboard: 10k visitors → 1k signups → 100 activated → 50 retained → 25 paying → 5 referring.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can't optimize all at once
- Metrics interdependent
- Need context for numbers

## Winner-Take-All Markets

**Principle**: Markets where top player captures disproportionate value (or entire market).

**When to Use**:
- Market strategy
- Competitive analysis
- Timing decisions
- Investment thesis

**Drivers**:
- Strong network effects
- High switching costs
- Economies of scale
- Standards/platforms

**Implications**:
- Be #1 or niche
- Speed matters enormously
- Subsidize growth
- Focus on dominance

**Example**: Google search; Facebook social; Amazon e-commerce; winner gets 70-90% market share.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all markets are winner-take-all
- Can lead to reckless growth
- Antitrust concerns
- Niche strategies still work

## Viral Coefficient (K-Factor)

**Principle**: Metric measuring how many new users each user brings (viral growth rate).

**When to Use**:
- Measuring virality
- Growth projections
- Product features prioritization
- Viral loop optimization

**Formula**: 
K = (Invites per User) × (Conversion Rate)

**Interpretation**:
- K > 1: Viral (exponential growth)
- K = 1: Replacement rate
- K < 1: Need other growth channels

**Example**: 
- User invites 5 friends × 20% accept = K = 1.0
- Need K > 1 for true virality
- Dropbox achieved K > 1 with storage incentives

**Questions**:
- How many invites per user?
- What's conversion rate?
- How to increase K?
- What's cycle time?

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to measure accurately
- Cycle time matters (fast > slow)
- Market saturation
- Quality vs quantity of users

## Foot-in-the-Door Effect

**Principle**: Small initial request increases likelihood of agreeing to larger request.

**When to Use**:
- Sales process
- Conversion optimization
- Behavior change
- Progressive commitment

**Mechanism**:
- Small yes → consistency pressure
- Self-perception changes ("I'm helpful")
- Commitment escalation
- Reduces psychological barriers

**Process**:
1. Small, easy request (hard to refuse)
2. Compliance
3. Larger request (related)
4. Higher compliance than direct ask

**Example**: 
- Free trial → paid subscription
- Email signup → purchase
- Survey → donation
- "Just 1 minute" → extended time

**Opposite**: Door-in-the-face (large → small)

**Pitfalls**:
- Can feel manipulative
- Initial request must succeed
- Requests must be related
- Awareness reduces effect

## Ben Franklin Effect

**Principle**: People who do favors for you end up liking you more (not less).

**When to Use**:
- Building relationships
- Networking
- Customer engagement
- Team dynamics

**Mechanism**:
- Cognitive dissonance: "Why would I help if I didn't like them?"
- Self-perception: "I helped, so I must like them"
- Investment creates commitment

**Process**:
1. Ask small favor
2. They comply
3. Justify behavior by liking you
4. Relationship improves

**Example**: 
- "Can you recommend a book?" → connection
- Customer feedback request → engagement
- Mentor asking protégé for advice
- Ben Franklin borrowing book from rival

**Questions**:
- What small favor can I ask?
- How to make helping easy?
- How to show appreciation?

**Pitfalls**:
- Request must be reasonable
- Can't be obviously manipulative
- Must genuinely appreciate
- Don't overuse
