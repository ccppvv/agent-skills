# Behavioral Economics Models

Models bridging psychology and economics - how humans actually make decisions vs rational actor assumptions.

## Loss Aversion

**Principle**: Losses loom larger than equivalent gains - the pain of losing $100 exceeds the pleasure of gaining $100.

**When to Use**:
- Framing decisions
- Risk assessment
- Incentive design

**Questions**:
- How can I frame this as avoiding loss vs gaining?
- Am I being overly loss-averse?
- What's the reference point determining "loss"?

**Example**: Endowment effect (overvaluing owned items), status quo bias, insurance popularity.

**Pitfalls**:
- Reference points are subjective
- Can lead to risk-seeking in losses
- Ratio varies (~2:1 to 2.5:1)

## Mental Accounting

**Principle**: People treat money differently depending on its source, purpose, or mental "account."

**When to Use**:
- Understanding spending behavior
- Savings strategy
- Budget design

**Questions**:
- What mental account is this in?
- Am I treating fungible resources as non-fungible?
- How does accounting affect decisions?

**Example**: Spending windfalls differently than salary, vacation budget vs emergency fund, house money effect.

**Pitfalls**:
- Money is actually fungible
- Can lead to suboptimal decisions
- Accounts are arbitrary

---

## Hyperbolic Discounting

**Principle**: People discount near-term rewards less steeply than distant rewards, preferring immediate gratification.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding procrastination
- Savings behavior
- Temptation management

**Questions**:
- Am I overvaluing immediate rewards?
- How can I commit my future self?
- What's the true discount rate?

**Example**: Preferring $100 today vs $110 tomorrow, but $110 in year vs $100 in 364 days; procrastination.

**Pitfalls**:
- Time-inconsistent preferences
- Leads to regret
- Hard to overcome

---

## Reference Dependence

**Principle**: Value is judged relative to a reference point, not absolute terms.

**When to Use**:
- Pricing strategy
- Satisfaction management
- Negotiation

**Questions**:
- What's the reference point?
- How was it set?
- Can I shift the reference?

**Example**: Salary negotiations (anchored to current salary), price comparisons, happiness adaptation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Reference points can be manipulated
- May ignore absolute values
- Adapts over time

---

## Choice Architecture

**Principle**: How choices are presented significantly affects decisions, even if options are identical.

**When to Use**:
- Product/service design
- Policy design
- Self-nudging

**Questions**:
- How is this choice structured?
- What's the default?
- How can architecture improve outcomes?

**Example**: Opt-in vs opt-out organ donation, retirement savings enrollment, cafeteria layout.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can be manipulative
- Effectiveness varies by domain
- May backfire if detected

---

## Default Effect

**Principle**: People disproportionately stick with pre-selected options.

**When to Use**:
- Defaults setting
- Inertia exploitation
- Behavior change

**Questions**:
- What's the current default?
- Should I change the default or override it?
- Is inertia helping or hurting?

**Example**: Printer settings, newsletter subscriptions, pension contributions.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can enable poor decisions
- Reduces active choice
- Not always ethical

---

## Present Bias

**Principle**: Overweighting present costs/benefits relative to future, beyond rational discounting.

**When to Use**:
- Procrastination understanding
- Commitment devices
- Long-term planning

**Questions**:
- Am I unduly focused on the present?
- What will future me regret?
- How can I pre-commit?

**Example**: Gym membership non-use, deadline crunching, unhealthy eating.

**Pitfalls**:
- Some immediacy is rational
- Commitment can be costly
- Future self may differ

---

## Temptation Bundling

**Principle**: Pairing unpleasant but beneficial activities with pleasant ones to increase follow-through.

**When to Use**:
- Habit formation
- Task completion
- Motivation boost

**Questions**:
- What do I want to do but shouldn't (yet)?
- What should I do but don't want to?
- How can I pair them?

**Example**: Only watching Netflix while exercising, audio books during commutes, social reward after work tasks.

**Pitfalls**:
- Temptation may dominate activity
- Not all pairings work
- Can create dependencies

---

## Commitment Contract

**Principle**: Self-imposed penalties for failing to meet goals increase follow-through.

**When to Use**:
- Self-control challenges
- Goal achievement
- Accountability

**Questions**:
- What penalty would actually motivate me?
- Who will enforce this?
- Is this commitment worth the cost?

**Example**: StickK contracts, public commitments, betting with friends.

**Pitfalls**:
- May feel coercive
- Penalty timing matters
- Can backfire psychologically

---

## Peak-End Rule

**Principle**: Experiences are judged by their peak (best/worst moment) and end, not average.

**When to Use**:
- Experience design
- Memory understanding
- Customer satisfaction

**Questions**:
- What's the peak moment?
- How does it end?
- Am I optimizing duration or peak-end?

**Example**: Medical procedures (end on pleasant note), customer service recovery, vacation memories.

**Pitfalls**:
- Duration neglect can be problematic
- May not apply to all experiences
- Can be manipulated

---

## Hedonic Adaptation

**Principle**: People return to baseline happiness despite positive/negative events (hedonic treadmill).

**When to Use**:
- Happiness strategy
- Purchase decisions
- Life planning

**Questions**:
- Will I adapt to this change?
- What provides lasting vs temporary satisfaction?
- Am I overestimating impact on happiness?

**Example**: Lottery winners return to baseline, new car excitement fades, lifestyle inflation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Adaptation varies by domain
- Some changes don't adapt (commuting, noise)
- Can lead to nihilism

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Cognitive Biases → Understanding irrational behavior
- Game Theory → Real human behavior in strategic contexts
- Decision Making → Practical decision frameworks
- Nudge Theory → Designing choice architecture

**Conflicts with**:
- Rational actor models
- Perfect self-control assumptions
- Time-consistent preferences
