# Information Theory Models

Models for understanding information, communication, and signal processing.

## Signal-to-Noise Ratio

**Principle**: The ratio of meaningful information to irrelevant data determines communication quality.

**When to Use**:
- Information filtering
- Communication strategy
- Data analysis

**Questions**:
- What's the actual signal vs background noise?
- How can we amplify signal or reduce noise?
- Are we drowning in data but starving for insight?

**Example**: News vs clickbait, key metrics vs vanity metrics, expert advice vs opinion.

**Pitfalls**:
- Signal is subjective (context-dependent)
- Filtering can remove weak but important signals
- May create echo chambers

---

## Redundancy

**Principle**: Duplication of critical information/components increases reliability but reduces efficiency.

**When to Use**:
- System reliability design
- Error correction
- Communication clarity

**Questions**:
- What's the cost of failure vs cost of redundancy?
- Where do we need backup systems?
- Is repetition adding clarity or clutter?

**Example**: RAID storage, DNA double helix, repeating key points in presentations.

**Pitfalls**:
- Excessive redundancy wastes resources
- May create complacency
- Doesn't help if failure mode is correlated

---

## Information Asymmetry

**Principle**: When one party has more/better information than another, it creates power imbalances and market inefficiencies.

**When to Use**:
- Negotiation strategy
- Market analysis
- Trust evaluation

**Questions**:
- Who knows what that others don't?
- How does information disparity affect incentives?
- What signals reveal hidden information?

**Example**: Used car sales (seller knows defects), job interviews (candidate knows abilities), insider trading.

**Pitfalls**:
- Assumes information matters (not always true)
- Hard to fully eliminate asymmetries
- Can lead to adversarial dynamics

---

## Filter Bubble

**Principle**: Personalized information filtering creates echo chambers that reinforce existing beliefs.

**When to Use**:
- Media consumption strategy
- Decision-making process
- Opinion formation

**Questions**:
- What perspectives am I not seeing?
- Is my information diet too homogeneous?
- How can I access contradictory views?

**Example**: Social media algorithms, search personalization, news source selection.

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to recognize from inside
- Complete exposure is impractical
- May lead to forced contrarianism

---

## Shannon Entropy

**Principle**: Measure of uncertainty/information content - more unpredictable = more information.

**When to Use**:
- Compression algorithms
- Measuring information value
- Understanding predictability

**Questions**:
- How much uncertainty does this resolve?
- What's the minimum information needed?
- How compressible is this data?

**Example**: ZIP compression, genetic information, weather forecasts reducing uncertainty.

**Pitfalls**:
- Abstract mathematical concept
- Doesn't account for semantic meaning
- High entropy ≠ high value

---

## Compression

**Principle**: Efficient representation of information by removing redundancy while preserving essentials.

**When to Use**:
- Communication efficiency
- Pattern recognition
- Abstraction creation

**Questions**:
- What's the essence we must preserve?
- What can be safely removed?
- What's lost in compression?

**Example**: Executive summaries, data compression algorithms, mental models themselves.

**Pitfalls**:
- Over-compression loses critical nuance
- Decompression may fail
- Assumes shared context

---

## Bandwidth

**Principle**: The maximum rate of information transfer through a channel.

**When to Use**:
- Communication channel selection
- Resource allocation
- Capacity planning

**Questions**:
- What's the capacity of this channel?
- Are we bandwidth-constrained?
- What's the right medium for this message?

**Example**: Face-to-face > video > phone > text for emotional communication; technical docs > conversation for complex specs.

**Pitfalls**:
- More bandwidth ≠ better communication
- Ignores latency and error rates
- May overlook channel-message fit

---

## Lossy vs Lossless Compression

**Principle**: Some compression preserves all information (lossless), other sacrifices details for efficiency (lossy).

**When to Use**:
- Data storage strategy
- Communication trade-offs
- Abstraction decisions

**Questions**:
- Can we tolerate information loss?
- What's the acceptable quality threshold?
- What's lost in simplification?

**Example**: Lossless: ZIP files, PNG images. Lossy: MP3 audio, JPEG images, executive summaries.

**Pitfalls**:
- Lossy compression is often irreversible
- Quality degradation may be imperceptible initially
- Accumulated losses compound

---

## Information Cascade

**Principle**: People's decisions are influenced by observing others' decisions, potentially overriding private information.

**When to Use**:
- Crowd behavior analysis
- Market dynamics
- Opinion formation

**Questions**:
- Are people acting on information or imitation?
- What triggered the cascade?
- How can independent judgment be preserved?

**Example**: Bank runs, fashion trends, investment bubbles, viral content.

**Pitfalls**:
- Hard to distinguish from legitimate consensus
- Early movers have disproportionate influence
- Can lead to herding toward wrong outcomes

---

## Overfitting

**Principle**: Models that fit data too closely capture noise rather than underlying patterns, failing to generalize.

**When to Use**:
- Model evaluation
- Pattern recognition
- Generalization assessment

**Questions**:
- Are we modeling signal or noise?
- Will this pattern hold out-of-sample?
- Is this too complex for the data?

**Example**: Machine learning models, historical pattern trading, conspiracy theories fitting random events.

**Pitfalls**:
- Underfitting is equally problematic
- Optimal complexity is hard to determine
- Hindsight makes overfitting seem reasonable

---

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Cognitive Biases → Understanding information processing failures
- Complexity Science → Dealing with high-entropy systems
- Bayesian Thinking → Updating beliefs with new information
- Game Theory → Strategic information revelation

**Conflicts with**:
- Perfect information assumptions
- Naive empiricism
- Over-reliance on intuition
