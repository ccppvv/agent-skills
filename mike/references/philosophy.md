# Philosophy & Social Science Models

## Occam's Razor
**Principle**: Simplest explanation usually correct.
**Application**: Problem diagnosis; theory selection; design.
**Example**: Don't multiply entities unnecessarily; prefer simple over complex.

## Falsifiability (Popper)
**Principle**: Scientific theories must be testable and potentially disprovable.
**Application**: Hypothesis testing; critical thinking; science vs. pseudoscience.
**Example**: "All swans are white" falsifiable by one black swan.

## Hanlon's Razor
**Principle**: Never attribute to malice what can be explained by incompetence.
**Application**: Conflict resolution; assumption checking; giving benefit of doubt.
**Example**: Mistakes more common than conspiracies; assume good intent first.

## Chesterton's Fence
**Principle**: Don't remove barrier until you know why it was erected.
**Application**: Change management; reform; understanding systems.
**Example**: Don't delete "useless" code; understand before changing; respect legacy.

## Veil of Ignorance (Rawls)
**Principle**: Design fair systems not knowing your position in them.
**Application**: Policy design; fairness testing; ethical decision-making.
**Example**: Social rules designed before knowing your role; unbiased systems.

## Tragedy of the Commons
**Principle**: Shared resources over-exploited without management.
**Application**: Resource management; collective action; sustainability.
**Example**: Overfishing; pollution; shared office resources.

## Collective Action Problem
**Principle**: Individual rational behavior leads to collective irrationality.
**Application**: Coordination; cooperation; social dilemmas.
**Example**: Prisoner's dilemma; voting paradoxes; free-riding.

## Social Contract
**Principle**: Implicit agreement on norms and rules for collective benefit.
**Application**: Governance; norms; cooperation.
**Example**: Laws we follow; cultural norms; implicit agreements.

## Virtue Ethics
**Principle**: Focus on character rather than rules or consequences.
**Application**: Leadership; culture; personal development.
**Example**: Cultivating virtues (courage, wisdom, justice); role models.

## Stoicism
**Principle**: Focus on what you can control; accept what you cannot.
**Application**: Resilience; stress management; perspective.
**Example**: Epictetus' dichotomy of control; emotional regulation; acceptance.

## Socratic Method

**Principle**: Question-based dialogue to expose assumptions and stimulate critical thinking.

**When to Use**:
- Teaching and learning
- Problem analysis
- Challenging assumptions
- Philosophical inquiry

**Process**:
1. Ask clarifying questions
2. Challenge assumptions
3. Examine evidence
4. Consider alternatives
5. Explore implications

**Questions**:
- What do you mean by that?
- How do you know that's true?
- What are you assuming?
- What if the opposite were true?

**Example**: Teacher asking "What is justice?" and using questions to help students discover deeper understanding rather than lecturing.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can seem confrontational
- Time-intensive
- Requires skill to guide effectively
- May frustrate those wanting direct answers

## Dialectics

**Principle**: Truth emerges through contradiction and synthesis of opposing ideas.

**When to Use**:
- Complex problem-solving
- Resolving conflicts
- Understanding paradoxes
- Theory development

**Structure** (Hegelian):
- **Thesis**: Initial idea
- **Antithesis**: Opposing idea
- **Synthesis**: Higher truth combining both

**Example**: Thesis: "Freedom is important." Antithesis: "Order is important." Synthesis: "Freedom within structures that maintain order."

**Pitfalls**:
- Can be overly abstract
- Not all contradictions resolve
- May force artificial synthesis

## Epistemology

**Principle**: Study of knowledge - how we know what we know.

**When to Use**:
- Evaluating claims
- Understanding belief formation
- Distinguishing knowledge from opinion
- Critical thinking

**Key Questions**:
- What counts as knowledge?
- How is knowledge acquired?
- What are the limits of knowledge?
- How do we justify beliefs?

**Sources of Knowledge**:
- Empiricism (experience)
- Rationalism (reason)
- Intuition
- Authority/testimony

**Example**: Scientific method based on empirical epistemology; mathematical proofs based on rational epistemology.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can lead to skepticism
- Different epistemologies conflict
- Philosophical rabbit holes

## Pragmatism

**Principle**: Truth is what works; judge ideas by their practical consequences.

**When to Use**:
- Evaluating theories
- Decision-making
- Avoiding abstract debates
- Action-oriented thinking

**Key Ideas**:
- Focus on effects, not origins
- Truth is provisional
- Knowledge is for action
- Test ideas empirically

**Questions**:
- What difference would it make?
- What are the practical consequences?
- Does it work?

**Example**: Don't debate abstract definitions; test solutions and keep what works. A/B testing embodies pragmatism.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can ignore deeper principles
- "Works" may be context-dependent
- Short-term vs long-term effects

## Existentialism

**Principle**: Existence precedes essence; humans create meaning through choices and actions.

**When to Use**:
- Understanding freedom and responsibility
- Dealing with meaninglessness
- Authentic decision-making
- Personal philosophy

**Key Concepts**:
- **Radical freedom**: You choose your essence
- **Responsibility**: Can't escape choice
- **Authenticity**: Living according to self
- **Absurdity**: Confronting meaninglessness

**Questions**:
- What am I choosing to become?
- Am I living authentically?
- What meaning do I create?

**Example**: Career choice not predetermined; you define yourself through actions. Can't blame circumstances for choices.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can be paralyzing
- Heavy existential burden
- Risk of nihilism

## Utilitarianism

**Principle**: Right action maximizes overall happiness/well-being for greatest number.

**When to Use**:
- Policy decisions
- Ethical dilemmas
- Resource allocation
- Balancing competing goods

**Forms**:
- **Act**: Evaluate each action
- **Rule**: Follow rules that maximize utility
- **Preference**: Satisfy preferences

**Questions**:
- What produces most good?
- For how many people?
- How do we measure well-being?

**Example**: Vaccine mandates justified if they maximize public health; taxation for social welfare.

**Pitfalls**:
- Measurement challenges
- May justify harming minorities
- Difficulty predicting consequences
- Ignores justice/rights concerns

---

## Categorical Imperative (Kant)

**Principle**: Act only according to maxims you could will to be universal laws.

**When to Use**:
- Ethical decision-making
- Testing moral rules
- Understanding duties
- Deontological ethics

**Formulations**:
1. **Universalizability**: Could everyone do this?
2. **Humanity**: Treat people as ends, not means
3. **Autonomy**: Act as if legislating for all

**Questions**:
- Would I want everyone to do this?
- Am I using someone merely as a means?
- Is this respectful of human dignity?

**Example**: Lying fails universalizability test (if everyone lied, communication breaks down). Don't deceive customers even if profitable.

**Pitfalls**:
- Rigid and absolute
- Conflicts between duties
- May ignore consequences
- Cultural variations

## Veil of Ignorance (Rawls)

**Principle**: Design fair systems as if you don't know your position in them.

**When to Use**:
- Creating fair policies
- Organizational design
- Testing for bias
- Justice considerations

**Thought Experiment**:
- Design society not knowing your:
  - Wealth, abilities, race, gender
  - Values, position, preferences
- Forces impartial perspective

**Questions**:
- Would I accept this not knowing my position?
- Does this protect the most vulnerable?
- Is this truly fair?

**Example**: Healthcare policy designed before knowing your health status; inheritance laws before knowing if you're rich.

**Pitfalls**:
- Still based on assumptions
- May be too conservative
- Difficult to truly achieve ignorance
- Cultural values still influence

## Consequentialism

**Principle**: Morality of actions determined solely by their consequences; right action produces best overall outcome.

**When to Use**:
- Utilitarian calculations
- Policy evaluation
- Outcome-focused decisions
- Balancing trade-offs

**Questions**:
- What are all the consequences?
- How do we weigh different outcomes?
- Short-term vs long-term effects?

**Example**: Lie to save a life; break promise if greater good results; ends justify means (when consequences warrant).

**Pitfalls**:
- Difficult to predict all consequences
- May justify terrible means
- Ignores intrinsic rights/duties
- Who decides "best" outcome?

## Rights-Based Ethics

**Principle**: Certain rights are fundamental and inviolable; actions are wrong if they violate rights, regardless of consequences.

**When to Use**:
- Human rights issues
- Policy design
- Ethical boundaries
- Protecting individuals

**Questions**:
- What rights are at stake?
- Do consequences justify rights violations?
- Whose rights take priority?

**Example**: Right to privacy, free speech, due process; can't torture even if it saves lives (in strict rights view).

**Pitfalls**:
- Rights can conflict
- Who defines which rights?
- Can be absolutist
- May ignore consequences entirely

---

## Care Ethics

**Principle**: Morality grounded in relationships, empathy, and responsibility for others; context and connection matter more than abstract rules.

**When to Use**:
- Personal relationships
- Healthcare decisions
- Social work
- Contextual ethics

**Questions**:
- How does this affect relationships?
- What does caring require here?
- Who is vulnerable and needs care?

**Example**: Caring for elderly parents; teacher adapting to student needs; medical decisions considering family impact.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can prioritize close relationships unfairly
- May lack universalizability
- Potentially reinforces gender stereotypes
- Risk of paternalism

---

## Moral Relativism

**Principle**: Moral truths are relative to cultures, societies, or individuals; no universal moral standards exist.

**When to Use**:
- Cross-cultural understanding
- Avoiding ethnocentrism
- Recognizing diversity
- Tolerance promotion

**Questions**:
- By whose standards?
- Are any values universal?
- Does context change morality?

**Example**: Dietary restrictions vary by culture; business ethics differ by country; historical moral standards evolve.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can justify anything ("it's their culture")
- Makes moral criticism difficult
- May excuse atrocities
- Contradicts itself (relativism is absolute?)

---

## Moral Hazard

**Principle**: Protection from consequences encourages riskier behavior; insulation from costs changes incentives.

**When to Use**:
- Insurance design
- Safety regulations
- Bailout policies
- Incentive structures

**Questions**:
- Does protection change behavior?
- Who bears the risk vs the cost?
- How do we maintain accountability?

**Example**: Bank bailouts encourage riskier lending; comprehensive insurance reduces precautions; no-fault systems and carelessness.

**Pitfalls**:
- Can be used to deny necessary protection
- Hard to measure behavioral change
- May blame victims unfairly
