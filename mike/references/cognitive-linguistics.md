# Cognitive Linguistics Models

Models exploring how language shapes thought and how thought shapes language.

## Conceptual Metaphor

**Principle**: Abstract concepts are understood through metaphors grounded in physical experience (e.g., "time is money", "ideas are food").

**When to Use**:
- Communication design
- Explaining complex ideas
- Understanding cultural framing
- Persuasive messaging

**Questions**:
- What metaphor underlies this concept?
- How does the metaphor shape understanding?
- What aspects does the metaphor hide?

**Example**: "Argument is war" → we attack positions, defend claims, shoot down ideas (vs "argument is dance" → collaborative exploration).

**Pitfalls**:
- Metaphors are culturally specific
- Can constrain thinking (dead metaphors become invisible)
- Multiple metaphors can conflict

## Prototype Theory

**Principle**: Categories organized around best examples (prototypes) rather than strict definitions; membership is graded, not binary.

**When to Use**:
- Category design
- User research
- Communication clarity
- Understanding ambiguity

**Questions**:
- What's the prototypical example?
- Where are the fuzzy boundaries?
- Who defines the prototype?

**Example**: Robin is prototypical "bird" (more than penguin or ostrich); tomato is technically fruit but psychologically vegetable.

**Pitfalls**:
- Prototypes vary by culture and experience
- Can perpetuate stereotypes
- Fuzzy boundaries create ambiguity

## Linguistic Relativity (Sapir-Whorf)

**Principle**: Language structure influences thought patterns and worldview; different languages encode different distinctions.

**When to Use**:
- Cross-cultural communication
- Product localization
- Understanding perspective differences
- Cognitive diversity

**Questions**:
- How does my language shape my thinking?
- What distinctions does this language force/prevent?
- How do speakers of other languages conceptualize this?

**Example**: Languages with grammatical gender affect object perception; color terms affect color discrimination; spatial reference frames affect navigation.

**Pitfalls**:
- Strong version (determinism) is overstated
- Thinking can transcend language
- Translation is possible (though not perfect)

## Embodied Cognition

**Principle**: Thinking is grounded in bodily experience and sensorimotor systems; cognition is not purely abstract symbol manipulation.

**When to Use**:
- UI/UX design
- Learning design
- Understanding intuition
- Gesture and embodied communication

**Questions**:
- What physical experience underlies this concept?
- How does body position affect thinking?
- What role do gestures play?

**Example**: "Grasping" ideas, "heavy" topics, "warm" personality; people actually feel warmer when holding warm drinks.

**Pitfalls**:
- Doesn't mean all thought is bodily
- Effects can be subtle
- Cultural variation in embodiment

## Frame Semantics

**Principle**: Words evoke mental frames (structured knowledge schemas) that shape interpretation; changing frames changes meaning.

**When to Use**:
- Messaging and framing
- Political communication
- Marketing
- Reframing problems

**Questions**:
- What frame does this language evoke?
- What's included/excluded by this frame?
- How can I reframe to shift perspective?

**Example**: "Tax relief" (taxes as burden) vs "tax investment" (taxes as contribution); "illegal immigrant" vs "undocumented worker".

**Pitfalls**:
- Frames are hard to change once activated
- Negating a frame reinforces it ("don't think of an elephant")
- Manipulation concerns

---

## Mental Spaces

**Principle**: We construct temporary conceptual domains (mental spaces) to reason about hypotheticals, possibilities, beliefs, and time.

**When to Use**:
- Scenario planning
- Counterfactual reasoning
- Understanding perspective-taking
- Narrative design

**Questions**:
- What mental space am I reasoning in?
- How do I map between spaces?
- What's reality vs hypothetical?

**Example**: "If I were rich, I'd..." (hypothetical space); "She thinks I'm wrong" (belief space); "Next year, we'll..." (future space).

**Pitfalls**:
- Easy to confuse spaces
- Mapping can be inconsistent
- Can lose track of what's real

## Conceptual Blending

**Principle**: New ideas emerge by blending elements from multiple mental spaces to create novel conceptual structures.

**When to Use**:
- Innovation
- Creative problem-solving
- Metaphor creation
- Understanding emergent concepts

**Questions**:
- What spaces are being blended?
- What emerges that wasn't in either input?
- What mappings enable the blend?

**Example**: "Computer virus" blends biological virus + computer program; "time zones" blend time + space; riddles blend incompatible frames.

**Pitfalls**:
- Not all blends are coherent
- Can be culturally specific
- May miss the blend mechanism

## Image Schemas

**Principle**: Basic recurring patterns from bodily experience (containers, paths, forces) structure abstract thought.

**When to Use**:
- Communication design
- UI/UX metaphors
- Understanding intuition
- Cross-domain reasoning

**Questions**:
- What spatial schema underlies this concept?
- How do physical experiences shape abstract thought?
- What image schema makes this intuitive?

**Example**: CONTAINER (in/out), PATH (from-to), FORCE (push/pull), BALANCE, UP-DOWN → "in love", "path to success", "under pressure".

**Pitfalls**:
- Schemas are prelinguistic (hard to articulate)
- Cultural variation exists
- Can constrain creative thinking

## Integration with Other Models

**Combines well with**:
- Framing → Language shaping perception
- Mental Models → Conceptual structures
- Systems Thinking → Holistic understanding
- Cultural Anthropology → Language and culture

**Conflicts with**:
- Pure logical reasoning (ignores embodiment)
- Language as transparent (ignores framing)
- Universal categories (ignores linguistic diversity)
